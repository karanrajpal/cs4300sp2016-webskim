    For Fintech Start-Ups, Efforts to Rethink Rules That Cramp Innovation

    By MICHAEL J. de la MERCEDAPRIL 6, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    SAN FRANCISCO — Getting a financial start-up off the ground is tough enough.

    The likelihood of incurring huge legal fees to secure money transmission licenses in all 50 states makes it that much harder. Yet that is what some digital currency start-ups face as a result of a bewildering hodgepodge of regulations across state and national lines that can make it difficult even to know which rules apply.

    Hoping to remove at least some of the burden, government agencies in the United States and elsewhere have been increasing their efforts to update the regulations.

    Last week, the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, an arm of the Treasury Department, unveiled a white paper that it said was a first step in helping foster innovation in financial tech, particularly for businesses tied to existing banks.

    “Not every innovation is appropriate for a regulated financial institution, and not every innovation that is appropriate for a regulated institution is appropriate for all regulated institutions,” Thomas J. Curry, the comptroller, said in remarks. “But avoiding new approaches completely is equally dangerous.”

    Many of the existing laws apply to older forms of finance like Western Union, the money-transfer company founded 165 years ago to capitalize on telegraph technology. The lack of fresh rules today means that modern fintech start-ups must also adapt to 19th-century regulations.

    And states have their own rules. In Wyoming, for instance, digital currency start-ups must maintain enormous amounts of capital reserves to operate there.

    “There is no regulation that is focused specifically on fintech,” said Jerry Brito, the executive director of Coin Center, a nonprofit advocacy group for digital currencies like Bitcoin. “What happens is that there are places where tech has outpaced the law.”

    The white paper follows some previous efforts by regulators to revise the rules. Last year, the Treasury Department sought information about marketplace lending, which matches borrowers and lenders over the Internet using algorithms.

    This year, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau outlined a new policy meant to give start-ups more confidence in introducing financial products without fear of a regulatory rebuke, at least initially.

    Existing rules make it difficult to guess which regulations apply to which firms. Take multisignature technology, in which wallets holding digital currencies like Bitcoin have authorization “keys” held by different parties. No transaction involving that money can take place without the consent of at least two of the key holders.

    Whether digital wallet companies can do anything with those deposits in the meantime, as insurance companies do when they invest their unpaid premiums, remains an open question.

    Some big banks, while arguing that they are embracing financial tech, want regulations for the new companies that are equal to what the banks face. Bank of America’s chief executive, Brian Moynihan, said at the World Economic Forum in Switzerland this year that disruptions to the payments system would be costly and that regulators must work to ensure that the industry can run smoothly.

    As William Winters, the chief of Standard Chartered Bank, put it during that panel, “As long as it’s a level playing field, it’s O.K.”

    For some companies in the fintech business, that is not a problem. Take Prosper, an early entrant in the marketplace-lending industry.

    The company has worked to comply with regulations on both sides of its business, its executives said. Investors in the loans it assembles are protected by the Securities and Exchange Commission, as well as regulators in 31 states.

    Borrowers on Prosper’s platform are covered by traditional banking rules, since Prosper technically teams with WebBank of Utah to disburse its loans.

    “It’s the same framework that applies to any bank lending program,” Sachin Adarkar, Prosper’s general counsel, said in an interview.

    Other governments have been starting their own initiatives. Britain unveiled Project Innovate last year to establish ways for start-ups to introduce services under the eye of government agencies.

    The European Union allows companies that hold a license in one country to “passport” it to other members of the bloc.

    Allowing something similar in the United States would most likely require action from Congress. Representative Patrick McHenry, a Republican from North Carolina, has said that Republicans plan to introduce legislation meant to modernize rules and let fintech innovation flourish. But analysts say that such a move could face resistance.

    “It would be politically difficult,” Mr. Brito said. “What you would be doing is challenging or removing authority of many different state regulators.”

    That makes it unlikely that the bramble of regulations will go away soon.
