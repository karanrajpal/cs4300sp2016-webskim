    Valeant Is a Reminder of the Peril of Outsize Executive Pay

    Peter Eavis @petereavis APRIL 4, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    There is no shortage of reasons for the spectacular rise and fall of Valeant Pharmaceuticals International.

    The firm’s strategy of hugely increasing the price of certain drugs eventually met stiff resistance from Congress. Its stock has fallen about 90 percent since August, as investors began to learn of accounting problems. The plunge in the stock also means Valeant cannot easily acquire other drug makers — a main source of growth.

    There’s another reason: the compensation of Valeant’s chief executive, J. Michael Pearson, and its senior executives, which most likely played an important role in driving the company’s behavior. Through shares and options acquired mostly from his compensation plans, Mr. Pearson owned nearly 3 percent of Valeant, a stake that was worth over $2.5 billion when the company’s stock was trading at its highs.

    The sheer size of Mr. Pearson’s holdings gives the impression that his package was uniquely aggressive. But in many ways it wasn’t.

    Mr. Pearson’s compensation had features that resembled those of other chief executives. The way in which Mr. Pearson was paid was held up as an example of how to create healthy incentives. As a result, Valeant’s debacle is a reminder of the subtle but harmful effect that outsize pay can have on other companies.

    As with any other public company, the details of Mr. Pearson’s pay can be found in proxy statements issued each year, which lay out the intricate methods that company boards have devised for tying pay to performance.

    These statements show that Mr. Pearson earned the most in long-term awards. The metric that determined these awards was something called total shareholder return, basically the performance of a company’s stock price, with any income from dividends added in.

    Paying a chief executive largely or solely on the basis of stock price performance might seem reckless. It would seem to create incentives for the executive to focus on actions that get impressive results for a year or two, rather than longer-term actions that might yield higher and more sustainable profits.

    But placing a heavy emphasis on the share price is a surprisingly common practice — and is supported by influential groups that advise shareholders on how to vote at annual meetings.

    The Conference Board, a research group, found that, for 2014, total shareholder return was used in 54 percent of long-term pay awards. And 31 percent had long-term plans that were determined wholly by the metric, though that percentage has fallen in recent years.

    Mr. Pearson’s long-term plan also had a feature that gave him a lot more shares as total shareholder return hit certain levels. His 2011 compensation agreement, providing a big payday for him, said that he would get zero “performance” shares if Valeant’s stock price did not go up by at least 15 percent a year over the following three years.

    Once he hit that threshold, he would get 120,000 shares. As the stock price hit other marks, he got more shares. The award topped out at a 60 percent-plus return, which would win him 480,000 shares.

    Ratcheting up pay like that is not uncommon. In 2014, 54 percent of companies surveyed by the Conference Board had maximum payouts that were 200 percent of the base award. Almost none exceeded 250 percent, so Mr. Pearson’s 300 percent reward was unusual.

    Mr. Pearson got something else in the long-term plan set in 2011 that appears to have made it particularly bountiful. The agreement included an outright grant of 500,000 stock options. An analysis by Kevin J. Murphy, a finance and economics professor at the University of Southern California, found that, under a certain range of stock prices, the options grant was significantly more generous than the performance share plan.

    The outright size of the options grant, and the price at which it started to pay off, is what made it more lucrative than the stock awards for the range of stock prices. (The stock awards became more valuable as the share price went up beyond this range, according to the analysis.)

    Few companies are like Valeant, pursuing the sort of growth-at-all-costs strategy that appeared to fell the firm. Still, there are plenty of signs that the drive for higher executive compensation propels a lot of the short-term thinking in American corporations. Companies that have raised many billions of dollars in the junk bond market in recent years have spent startlingly little of it on new plants and operations — things that prime future growth and provide more jobs. It went instead to paying back more expensive debts, and to financing acquisitions and private equity deals.

    Companies are currently spending about $450 billion a year buying back shares. This helps chief executives increase the company’s earnings per share, a measure of profits that shareholders focus on obsessively. Conveniently, it is often a measure used to help set executive pay.

    The impact of buybacks on earnings per share is not small; Deutsche Bank calculates that in recent years about a quarter of per-share earnings growth has come from using buybacks to reduce the amount of shares outstanding.

    And private companies, where executive pay is not tied to the performance of publicly traded shares, invest more than their public peers, according to some economists.

    But Valeant also raises the issues of skewed incentives and inequality. Mr. Pearson did put some of his own money into Valeant, a requirement that was supposed to make him more accountable. But those sums were tiny in comparison with what the company paid him.

    And Mr. Pearson’s pay is a stark reminder of how executive pay has helped widen wage differences. Analysts from the A.F.L.-C.I.O., a federation of unions, estimated that chief executive pay was 331 times as much as that of ordinary workers in 2013, up from 46 times in the 1980s. In his best-selling book on inequality, “Capital in the Twenty-First Century,” Thomas Piketty placed much blame for rising income differences on the lucrative pay packages given to senior executives year after year.

    And there seem few ways to stop the seemingly inexorable rise of executive pay.

    Imposing significantly higher taxes on the highest earners is the other way to get at the problem, since companies might not pay as much if they knew that, say, 70 percent of the compensation was going to the government. But Congress shows zero appetite for taxing at that level.

    Shareholders support all the elaborate efforts that aim to bring accountability to compensation. Supporters of such measures as pay for performance might even contend that Mr. Pearson got his comeuppance, since his wealth is now a fraction of what it was, after the plunge in Valeant’s shares. But even at these bombed-out levels, his effective holdings appear to be worth more than $200 million.
