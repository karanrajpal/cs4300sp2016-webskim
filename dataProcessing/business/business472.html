    Puerto Rico’s Prosperous D.C. Power Couple

    By ERIC LIPTON and MICHAEL CORKERYAPRIL 12, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    WASHINGTON — As Puerto Rico has spiraled toward possible bankruptcy, the island’s sole representative in Congress has seen his family wealth swell, thanks in part to Wall Street companies that have sought to capitalize on the island’s financial crisis and have hired his wife to advise them.

    The dual roles — those of a lawmaker and a spouse who are both involved in the financial affairs of their community — are hardly unusual in Washington, a city where power couples are increasingly common and, at times, celebrated.

    But legislation that Pedro R. Pierluisi, a Democrat and the resident commissioner to Congress from Puerto Rico, has introduced would benefit at least two of the companies that have hired his wife, María Elena Carrión, for financial advice.

    Ms. Carrión set up her own Puerto Rico-based financial advisory firm just 20 days after Mr. Pierluisi, 56, was elected to Congress in 2008, records show. Its value has climbed to more than $1 million since, and the couple’s average net worth has increased 27-fold since that election.

    And as Ms. Carrión, 51, has done her work for these firms, Mr. Pierluisi’s office has been lobbied by two of her clients, Fundamental Advisors and Och-Ziff Capital Management, documents provided by the resident commissioner’s office show.

    Separately, a Wall Street hedge fund executive whose firm took part in a $3.5 billion bond sale in 2014 for Puerto Rico, has raised money for Mr. Pierluisi’s race for governor of Puerto Rico, campaign finance records and emails show.

    “The congressman would like to continue the dialogue with the investment community,” said an email invitation the executive sent in July 2014. It added that “other asset managers and political personalities” would be attending the event. “A contribution is suggested, as the congressman is pursuing the governorship in 2016.”

    No House rules require Mr. Pierluisi to disclose the names of his wife’s clients, and he did not. The names of her Wall Street clients came to light after The New York Times requested them.

    In separate interviews, both Mr. Pierluisi and Ms. Carrión said they kept their professional careers separate, and that nothing that Mr. Pierluisi had done in Congress had in any way been intended to benefit his wife’s start-up company, Multicultural Capital, or his bid for governor.

    “She has every right to engage in her profession,” Mr. Pierluisi said during an interview at his Capitol Hill office. “It has nothing to do with what I am doing in Congress. The only link here is that it happens in Puerto Rico.”

    Ms. Carrión — who earned a master’s degree in business administration from Columbia University and spent more than a decade working for Wall Street investment banks — called questions about the potential conflict of interest unfounded.

    “I’m a mergers and acquisitions specialist with 30 years experience in the financial sector,” she said in a written statement. “As a woman and a minority, I’m proud to own my own business.”

    Executives at the firms that hired her, including Fundamental Advisors and Och-Ziff, declined to comment on the record.

    House ethics rules place no limits on the work that a spouse of a lawmaker can do. Two ethics lawyers who examined the matter at the request of The Times said they saw no violation of the rules based on the work that Ms. Carrión has done.

    But Meredith McGehee, policy director at the Campaign Legal Center, a campaign finance watchdog group, said that Mr. Pierluisi should have disclosed the deals in which his wife had been involved.

    “Democracy is a confidence game — people need to have confidence that elected officials did not come into power to enrich themselves,” Ms. McGehee said. “The reality is what they have done here has created some smoke, the appearance of a conflict, no matter how much the congressman protests that assertion.”

    Ms. Carrión describes Multicultural Capital as an “independent consulting boutique” that helps companies interested in investing in Puerto Rico and other Latino markets find investment properties worth buying.

    When she opened the business after her husband’s election, it represented a return to the financial world for Ms. Carrión, an expert in emerging markets who had worked for 15 years at Wall Street firms like Prudential Financial and Bankers Trust before taking a break to pursue a doctorate in Puerto Rican history.

    From her small office in San Juan, Ms. Carrión fielded requests from investors looking to buy or sell assets, including hotels and residential buildings.

    Her work in this area has coincided with a surge in interest in the island by major Wall Street players, like Apollo Global Management and Och-Ziff Capital Management, two New York-based investment funds. They went to Puerto Rico looking for bargains on so-called distressed assets, such as struggling hotels, or to buy some of the island’s public debt, because the government was offering unusually high interest rates to pull in much-needed money.

    Och-Ziff asked for Ms. Carrión’s help in 2012 and 2014 as it looked for investments, eventually purchasing stakes in a hotel and residential complex on the island. Ms. Carrión’s firm earned a fee of more than $1 million for helping set up the deal, according to participants in the transaction. She would not say how much her company earned in fees.

    More recently, Ms. Carrión was hired by Fundamental Advisors. Her company was not able to help find a suitable investment, but her fee was in the tens of thousands of dollars before the contract was terminated at the end of last year.

    In March 2014, Och-Ziff was one of at least a dozen hedge funds that purchased large stakes in $3.5 billion worth of bonds sold by the Puerto Rican government, with the help of Wall Street firms including Morgan Stanley.

    This bond offering, the largest sale of junk-rated municipal debt in United States history, attracted hedge funds instead of the more traditional municipal bond investors because of the unusually high interest rates that the strapped Puerto Rican government was willing to offer.

    “It is a very important event for Puerto Rico,” Hector Negroni, chief investment officer at the hedge fund run by Fundamental Advisors, told The Financial Times on the day of the sale. “The size of the deal, the audience they are targeting and the price they are paying acknowledge the depth of Puerto Rico’s liquidity issues.”

    Within months of the 2014 bond sale, Wall Street executives involved in the transaction turned to Mr. Pierluisi’s office for help. He had introduced legislation first in July 2014 and again in February 2015 that would allow the Puerto Rican government to take advantage of United States bankruptcy provisions available to the 50 states.

    For companies like Och-Ziff and Fundamental Advisors, that change in the law would protect their investments in Puerto Rico general obligation bonds if the island won bankruptcy protection, while holders of other debts issued by the Puerto Rican government would be paid back less than promised.

    “The congressman took this position for one reason and one reason only — because he believes it is in the best interests of the 3.5 million American citizens that he represents,” a statement from his office said.

    But in August 2014, Julio A. Cabral-Corrada, then a broker at Morgan Stanley, which had helped issue the $3.5 billion in debt, organized a fund-raising event at the Peninsula Hotel on Fifth Avenue in New York, openly hinting in the email invitation that attendees would have access to Mr. Pierluisi “to continue the dialogue with the investment community” as the negotiations in Congress continued.

    House members are not allowed to “sponsor or participate in any solicitation that offers donors any special access,” the rules say.

    Mr. Cabral-Corrada, in an interview, declined to discuss the event or those who attended.

    Campaign finance records — for Mr. Pierluisi’s House race and his account in Puerto Rico to fund his bid for governor — show contributions around the time of the event from more than a dozen financial industry figures and lobbyists.

    Mr. Pierluisi, provided with a copy of the fund-raising invitation, said he had not previously seen it.

    “This is unfortunate language,” he said.

    Congress has not taken any action on legislation intended to help resolve Puerto Rico’s financial crisis. A new proposal emerged late last month that would no longer guarantee that general obligation bondholders were fully protected. In the interview, Mr. Pierluisi offered his tentative support for part of this new House proposal, which would replace his own legislative plan, as further proof that he was not trying to help any of his wife’s former clients.

    Ms. Carrión added that she had never worked for the hedge fund divisions of the firms that invested in bonds, but instead helped their private equity wings, which purchase real estate assets like hotels, and that her contracts with all of her clients included a provision making clear she does not lobby Congress.

    Some of the firms are so small, though, that executives are involved in running both divisions out of the same offices.

    In the time since Mr. Pierluisi has been in office, his wife’s firm has grown from having no revenue to being worth more than $1 million, his most recent financial disclosure report, filed in 2015, says.

    Financial disclosure reports Mr. Pierluisi filed with the House show his family’s average net worth, subtracting debts from assets, surged to $1 million in 2014, the most recent report, from $36,501 in 2008, the year he was elected.

    Mr. Pierluisi said the change in his family wealth was entirely a result of his wife’s earnings.

    In Puerto Rico, some see a familiar pattern. Emilio Pantojas García, a professor at the University of Puerto Rico who has written extensively on the island’s debt crisis, said that even if Ms. Carrión’s work had not benefited from Mr. Pierluisi’s presence in Congress, the arrangement was disturbing.

    “While everyone else suffers, they are profiting from the crisis,” he said. “Unfortunately, it is par for the course when it comes to the political elite in Puerto Rico.”

    Eric Lipton reported from Washington, and Michael Corkery from New York.
