    Ten Key Questions on Health Care Enrollment

    By WILSON ANDREWS, HAEYOUN PARK and ARCHIE TSE MARCH 27, 2014

    Share Your Experience With the Affordable Care Act

    If you are uninsured but have not yet signed up for health insurance, or do not intend to, we invite you to tell us your story.

    Q. What happens on Monday?
    A. Monday is the last day for most people to sign up for health insurance through the exchanges for coverage this year. The Obama administration said this week that people who tried to apply but were blocked by technical problems with the federal exchange — and some other groups — would be given more time. Some state exchanges have also said they would extend the deadline.

     A. The White House said on Thursday that more than six million people had signed up for private plans, exceeding the administration’s revised goal, and health officials are betting on a last-minute surge. The administration’s initial target was seven million enrolled.
    Q. Is everyone who signed up now covered?
    A. Not necessarily. Federal officials say they do not know how many people have actually paid their first month’s insurance premium, which is what is needed for coverage to begin. According to some insurance companies, 15 percent to 20 percent of people who signed up have not paid their premiums.
    Q. Did the health care law reduce the number of uninsured?
    A. Many signs point to a reduction in the uninsured — including enrollment in exchanges, Medicaid sign-ups and anecdotal reports from insurers. But reliable figures are not available at this time.

     A. States that ran their own exchanges generally did better than states that used HealthCare.gov, when enrollment is measured as a fraction of the potential market, which is the pool of people eligible to sign up for private insurance in each state. But some state exchanges, like the federal exchange, were plagued by technical problems.
    Q. Which states fared best?
    A. Best: Eight states signed up 20 percent or more of the potential market for private insurance. Of these, the top four states have Democratic governors and set up their own exchanges. Vermont, clearly a leader among this group, has long been hospitable to universal health care.

     A. About a quarter of the people who enrolled are between 18 and 34. But some health care experts, like Larry Levitt at Kaiser Family Foundation, say that 40 percent of those 18 to 34 — that age group’s share of potential enrollees — is what would be ideal to keep premiums down. Young people usually have lower medical costs, and their premiums help pay for the higher cost of insuring older, sicker people.

     A. More than 60 percent of the enrollees chose a silver plan, which pays for about 70 percent of health costs. Plans were divided into categories that indicated the generosity of coverage. The platinum plan has the most coverage, but is often more expensive.
    Q. How many people got coverage because of the Medicaid expansion?
    A. We will have to wait for the states to release their first quarter reports, which will show enrollment data. About half the states decided to expand Medicaid coverage in 2014 to adults with incomes below 138 percent of the federal poverty line.
    Q. Will my premium go up next year?
    A. It is hard to know. Insurers are just beginning to assess 2014 claims experience, a major factor they will consider in setting premiums for 2015. Premiums are not expected to spike next year, but insurers say they will be somewhat higher because of many last-minute changes in government rules and policy. Premium increases, like premiums themselves, will vary from state to state.

    Note: The eligible population includes legal residents who are uninsured or who buy plans on the individual market, have incomes above Medicaid/Children’s Health Insurance Program eligibility levels, and who do not have access to employer-sponsored coverage.
