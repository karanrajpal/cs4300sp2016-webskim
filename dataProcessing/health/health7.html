    Navigating School With Diabetes

    By CATHERINE SAINT LOUIS JAN. 20, 2016 Related Article

    In October, The Times reported on students with Type 1 diabetes who struggle to get medical care while they are in school. In spite of federal protections, diabetic students have been barred from neighborhood schools and from participating in extracurricular activities.

    We asked parents of children with Type 1 diabetes to share their stories about the educational obstacles they have encountered and the steps they took to overcome them. Here are a few of their responses, edited and condensed for space.

    1. The Parent Becomes the Teacher
      Paige Wichman, Tennessee

      Our small private school doesn’t have a nurse, so everything has to be handled by my daughter Olivia’s teachers. I was actually terrified that we wouldn’t be accepted. I wondered if they wouldn’t want to deal with her diabetes. But they didn’t bat an eye.

      Before kindergarten, my husband and I sat down around a table and hashed it out with the head of school, her teacher-to-be and the class assistant. It was obvious in the meeting that her assigned teacher felt she might not be the best fit for Olivia.

      So Olivia was switched to another class with a teacher and assistant who were less intimidated by her insulin pump and more comfortable with what she needed. I appreciated the teacher’s honesty.

      I conducted trainings with teachers and staff and have maintained near-constant contact with them during the day. I eventually worked my way into a job at the school in community development, and now I’m on campus every day. Building a relationship with this school was a big benefit for my family, for Olivia and for the community. Everyone knows about her illness, and it’s a part of everyday routine.

    2. Don’t Expect the School to Know Anything
      Linda Gervais, Maine

      Five years ago, at age 8, our daughter Eliza was diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes. I didn’t think I had to do any research. We thought the district would already have in place appropriate medical care for a newly diagnosed child.

      But ultimately, we had to learn every Maine law and federal law regarding Americans with disabilities to advocate for appropriate care at school.

      There is no nurse at our elementary school, just a part-time nurse who goes between schools. In a meeting, we were told the school secretary would give her shots. It was terrifying. It was not the secretary we were scared of, just the sheer amount of responsibility she had in a large school district. Also, she had not volunteered. The head of nursing said that’s what other secretaries had done in other schools, so that’s what she could do.

      We were told a group of other people would be trained to give Eliza insulin shots when she needed them. Our concern was it would be a different person each time. I contacted the American Diabetes Association when we got home.

      We should have been figuring out our new normal as a family. Instead, we were fighting to make sure she’s safe and thriving in school, which we wouldn’t have thought we’d have to do. It was disappointing. I ended up sitting outside the superintendent’s office to get a meeting. We weren’t asking for a nurse; we were asking for a consistent person. He was very helpful.

      Eventually, they hired a full-time health aide. It was not just my daughter who benefited. This incredible woman helped the whole school. She’s now part of the family. She visits us in the summer, and she’s another grandmother to our daughter.
    4. Become the Squeaky Wheel
      Wendy Foster, Ohio

      Unfortunately, I just had to be available all the time. I had to be available to go on every field trip. And the school nurses were totally uneducated on Type 1; they always treated it as Type 2, and I would have arguments over treatment of lows and highs.

      A lot of people would give him sugar-free foods thinking they didn’t have any carbs. One time a teacher brought in a giant plastic pumpkin full of sugar-free candy. There were more carbohydrates in each one than in candy with sugar.

      Thank goodness my son was never shy and was his best advocate. I also became a major squeaky wheel. There were plenty of people who stopped talking to me because I totally had called them out. You can’t be shy when your child has diabetes.

    5. Be Respectfully Bold
      Eric Foster, Ohio

      I’m 19. I was fairly confident by fifth grade. I’d had Type 1 diabetes for about seven years by then. I knew how to handle low and high blood sugar. I’m definitely proud of my mother and love her for what she’s done. She was my bulldozer. She’s got me through a lot.

      Once in high school, I had an insulin pump. The pump’s cord wasn’t long enough for me to tuck my shirt in and have it be comfortable. My parents sent me to private Catholic school. We had to wear dress pants, and the appropriate polo from the school that had to be tucked in.

      So a substitute teacher said, “Hey Eric, tuck your shirt in.”

      I replied: “I can’t. The tube isn’t long enough. I just need to leave my shirt out on this side.”

      He said: “I don’t care. My nurse has a pump, and she can tuck her shirt in.”

      I tried to defend myself again, and said, “I can’t tuck my shirt in.”

      Then he asked to talk to me after class and said, “Eric, you need to stop using diabetes as a crutch to get through your life.”

      It was very insulting. I was a junior in high school. I was really angry.

      But I would say there’s an upside to having diabetes. It’s taught me to be more patient with people. You need to keep telling yourself, “People are doing what they think is best, and even if you know it’s not.” You have to be respectfully bold and say, “Ma’am, what you’re saying is wrong.” Be confident in what you know about your diabetes.


    6. Lobby for Change
      Haley Karish, California

      My daughter Kate was diagnosed before kindergarten. That was in 2012, before the court decision in California that said that non-nurses could administer insulin.

      I have been very pleased with Kate’s care. But that particular year began weakly. I felt our nurse was stretched too thin. She was covering three schools. My daughter was waiting to eat lunch, because no one else can give her insulin except for the nurse, even though my daughter wears an insulin pump.

      I made her lunch and counted the carbs, so it’s about pressing the button on her pump. If she ate the school lunch, as some kids do, I understand why a nurse might be needed.

      I said, “Something has to change” to the director of health services, whom I really like. As it turns out, they were in the process of hiring a health clerk for one of those three schools. So that enabled the nurse to be able to be more present at my daughter’s school.

      Since kindergarten, we’ve had good communication between nursing staff and myself. They are just on it. When she goes low, she checks herself. She’s old enough now at 8 that she presses the button on the insulin pump herself with some oversight by the health aide.

      No one has ever told us what school she has to go to. She is excluded from nothing. Unless she has low blood sugar, she can do everything other kids can do, like playing kickball, running around, playing tag.


    7. Switch Schools if Necessary
      Samantha Hain, New York

      I have two children with Type 1 diabetes. Last year in public school, my son, David – then a first grader – was told at least a half-dozen times not to come to school because there would be nobody there to give insulin.

      In New York, only nurses can legally provide insulin. The nurse was out and they didn’t have a replacement in the building. There were times I had to pick him up because there was nobody there to dose him.

      I’m a writer, and I freelance from home, but there were a lot of days I couldn’t work. I can’t be in two places at once, and my priority is making sure my child is O.K. We had a 504 plan and it wasn’t honored.

      David did have a one-on-one aide. She was phenomenal, but she was not qualified to check blood sugar or to give insulin. She was just there to watch his carbs, to watch what he ate and to make sure he didn’t pass out. Unfortunately, Type 1 diabetes is an extremely misunderstood disease. After David graduated, I chose not to send my daughter there. I opted for a private school.


    9. Disinvited to the Party
      Melissa Madero, Texas

      Fabian was diagnosed in July 2011. He was 7, about to turn 8. At that time we were in Georgia. There, we had an amazing nurse whose father was diabetic as well.

      We came to Texas in 2013. Immediately when we registered for public school, it was clear he was a burden. We had a school nurse twice a week, and a school aide was there five days a week.

      Still, they had him going into the boys’ bathroom doing his injection unsupervised. It states in his doctor’s orders that he has to be supervised, and he wasn’t. They were constantly having me pick him up from school every time he had a low or a high. Most weeks, I had to pick him up.

      I would take him back to work with me; I was working in marketing then. I ended up just having to quit.

      Right before last Christmas break, they had Fabian go to the

      nurse’s office and sit there during the class Christmas party. Other kids were all having cupcakes. They said they didn’t know if he could have a cupcake. He was 9 then. He came home and told me immediately what had happened. I emailed the school nurse, and she replied that she didn’t know if he could have sugar or more insulin. Yet it specifies in doctor’s orders that he’s allowed to attend any party. It also says that for any additional carbs he eats, he needs insulin. I just don’t think they want to deal with him.

    10. Why Hold It Against Us?
      Jalissa Gascho, Michigan

      I am a Type 1 diabetic. I’m 26 now. My elementary school had a full-time nurse, and she was there all day. I can’t imagine sending my child to a place where they don’t have a nurse all the time; that’s mind-boggling.

      I went to public schools in a small farming community. As a child, I was blessed because my mother, a nurse, was willing to educate all of my educators. She had the time to come to school if I needed an injection. Parents today can’t necessarily do that.

      By middle school, I kept everything in my locker, and then before lunch I would run to the locker. I had a syringe and a vial of insulin. At that point, I wasn’t using the pens – that was the way it was back in the day. I kept my glucose meter with me all the time, in a bag. I carried that and a juice box in case my blood sugar went low.

      Now some schools won’t let kids carry a backpack anymore. When your blood sugar goes low, the last thing you want to do is walk anywhere.

      It’s amazing to me that schools are unwilling to adapt and learn how to care for such an increasingly common disease. We never asked for this. How could you possibly hold it against us?
