    On Doping or Sponsors, a Vocal Runner Holds Track Officials’ Feet to the Fire

    By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSAPRIL 2, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    The outspoken middle-distance runner Nick Symmonds tricks himself into believing that doping is not going on in his event. Otherwise, he could not climb out of bed every morning to train.

    “I stick my head in the sand and pretend it’s not happening,” Symmonds, an American 800-meter standout, said recently in a telephone interview. “It’s a filthy, dirty sport — the cheaters are always going to be two steps ahead of the testers. It’s never a level playing field.

    “Anyone who stands on the starting line at an Olympic final and thinks they’re racing against a clean field is delusional.”

    Symmonds does not shy away from voicing his opinion on track’s inequities or from keeping pressure on those responsible for making decisions in a sport that has been engulfed by doping scandals with the Olympics in Rio de Janeiro approaching.

    He boycotted the world track and field championships in Beijing last summer to make a point about the relationships among athletes, sponsors and national bodies. Soon, he is headed to court as he takes on USA Track & Field, along with the United States Olympic Committee, over the types of sponsors that can be displayed on uniforms and other clothing at the Olympic trials in July.

    “Track needs a total rehaul,” he said. “It’s broken beyond repair at the moment.”

    Symmonds did not compete two weeks ago at the world indoor championships in Portland, Ore. — he has stopped racing indoors — but appreciated that track’s international governing body excluded Russians over doping charges. The International Association of Athletics Federations, track’s governing body, could decide in late May or early June whether Russia’s track and field program has done enough to repair its antidoping measures and can be reinstated for the Rio Games.

    “Now that they’re trying to catch them, I feel better,” said Symmonds, a two-time Olympian who lives in Seattle and had been doing high-altitude workouts in Albuquerque as he recovered from a torn left calf. “But it’s still a long, long way from cleaning up the sport.”

    Sebastian Coe, the president of the I.A.A.F., has a simple message for clean athletes: Help catch the cheaters.

    “These days have got to be over, where clean athletes sit there and say, ‘There’s no system,’ ” Coe said, adding that by speaking out, athletes could speed up the process of catching and punishing dopers.

    Symmonds has never kept his thoughts to himself.

    On his Twitter account last week, he wrote: “Billions of dollars in ad revenue and athletes are paid $0 directly. Olympians are indentured servants of the IOC.” He posted a link to a story with the headline “NBC on Track to Shatter Olympics Ad Sales Record.”

    After making the world team last summer, Symmonds took a hard-line stance over what he called a restrictive, unclear policy written by U.S.A.T.F. regarding exactly when American athletes were required to wear team-sponsored Nike gear while in Beijing. Symmonds, 32, is sponsored by a rival shoe company, Brooks, and wanted it clearly detailed what constituted a team function.

    With no resolution, he stayed home for the world championships. He watched the 800 final and thought he could have won a medal.

    “But I haven’t regretted it for two seconds,” said Symmonds, who dedicated his silver medal at the 2013 world championships in Moscow to his gay and lesbian friends as a show of support amid Russia’s antigay legislation.

    Now, Symmonds and a company he co-founded, Run Gum, are in the middle of a lawsuit with U.S.A.T.F. and the U.S.O.C., attempting to break a monopoly held by certain sponsors. In part, the suit states: “U.S.A.T.F., the U.S.O.C., and their co-conspirators cannot curtail competition by picking and choosing eligible market participants and excluding the rest.”

    The U.S.A.T.F. had no comment on the lawsuit. The U.S.O.C. filed a motion to dismiss the case, which will be heard in about two weeks.

    On his website, Symmonds describes himself as not only an Olympian but an Eagle Scout and a professional adventurer. He recently went fishing on the Willamette River in Oregon, posting photos of the sturgeon he caught before releasing them.

    “If you love em, let them go,” he wrote.

    Symmonds will not let track off the hook so easily.

    “Of course, doping is the most immediate concern,” Symmonds said, adding that he wanted to know if Coe planned to “revitalize the sport, how you repackage it and make it palatable to a 21st-century audience.”

    “Right now, nobody wants to watch track and field,” he said.
