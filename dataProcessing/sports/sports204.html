    Twitter Gains Rights to Stream Thursday N.F.L. Games

    By KEN BELSON and MIKE ISAACAPRIL 5, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Leave it to the N.F.L. to sell the same product three times.

    A decade ago, the league began showing Thursday night games on its fledgling NFL Network. Then CBS, and later NBC, agreed to pay the league hundreds of millions of dollars a year for the right to broadcast some of those games as well.

    On Tuesday, the N.F.L. said that Twitter will join the party. Eager to offset slowing growth on its flagship micro-blogging platform, Twitter will pay about $15 million to stream 10 Thursday night games this season.

    The games shown on Twitter will be the CBS or NBC broadcasts, depending on which network is showing the game, and will include pre- and postgame highlights only available on Twitter. Twitter and the N.F.L. have not announced how the games will look on individual feeds.

    The partnership speaks volumes about the power of the country’s most powerful sports league, which wants to expand its footprint overseas and reach younger consumers who may never pay for a cable or satellite package, or even own a television.

    It also illuminates the sharp-elbowed race to survive in Silicon Valley, where Twitter and other technology companies like Facebook, Amazon and Netflix are rushing to find ways to offer consumers must-see content everywhere and at any time. By joining a sports behemoth that garners the highest ratings in television, Twitter hopes to pump air back into its sagging fortunes.

    “They need to give people more reasons to come back to Twitter to get their info-of-the-day fix,” said Eric Jackson, managing director of SpringOwl Asset Management and a close watcher of the company. “If these N.F.L. games go well, they can expand into other types of content and give users more reasons to spend more time on Twitter properties each day.”

    Major League Baseball, the N.B.A. and other sports leagues already stream national packages of games for $100 or more a season. But they, like the N.F.L., will be watching to see how fans consume games on their smartphones and tablets and whether the alliance with Twitter can generate profits.

    Even as the nation’s largest sports league with $13 billion in annual revenue, the N.F.L. is trying to anticipate shifts in the media landscape. For decades, the league has relied on a tested model: Collect billions of dollars from television networks that broadcast games for free over the air, or from cable and satellite networks.

    Most of the league’s television agreements with these networks expire in the next five years, so the N.F.L. wants to find new ways to market its games for the next generation of fans.

    “There are huge swaths of young people who never watch broadcast television,” said Ed Desser, a sports media consultant. The streaming experiment “allows them to plan ahead.”

    For now, along with appearing on Twitter’s mobile and desktop apps, the company said it will show games on video game consoles that use Twitter apps, such as Microsoft’s Xbox.

    At the N.F.L.’s annual meeting last month, league executives told team owners and executives that the biggest opportunities for new revenue would come from digital and international businesses. Selling the streaming rights to live games to Twitter appears to fill both of those categories, without upsetting CBS, NBC and other networks that pay dearly for media rights.

    To assuage them, Twitter will show the networks’ ads. But to make money it will sell ads for the time normally reserved for local spots. This let Twitter pay less than it might have otherwise, and it will allow NBC and CBS to reach more viewers.

    “The attraction wasn’t just revenue, it was a reach decision,” said Joe Lockhart, a league spokesman. Other bidders, he added, had offered more money than Twitter.

    By signing a one-year deal with Twitter, the N.F.L. will retain the flexibility to look elsewhere for the long run.

    Roger Goodell, the N.F.L.’s commissioner, announced the deal, appropriately enough, on his Twitter feed, his first Tweet in 19 months.

    Twitter is eager to push further into the sports world to attract new users. Despite increasing competition from Facebook’s promoting sports-related discussion on its platform, Twitter has long been the online forum of choice for sports fans, journalists and athletes on game day.

    The league might have had a receptive ear at Twitter. The company’s chief financial officer, Anthony Noto, held the same position at the N.F.L. from February 2008 to October 2010.

    In February, the league sold the rights to Thursday Night Football to CBS and NBC in a two-year deal worth an estimated $450 million annually. Thursday night games will continue to be shown on the NFL Network as well.

    The league then went looking for a partner to stream the games, and Twitter beat out other digital media companies for the rights, including Facebook, which made its interest in the deal public last month, as well as Amazon, according to a person directly involved in the talks who requested anonymity because the terms of the negotiations were private.

    The move is a significant strategic shift for Twitter, which last month celebrated its 10th anniversary. While Twitter has grown to hundreds of millions of regular users, for years the company has been on a quest to boost its slowing growth by making significant changes to the product and by spending millions in marketing campaigns.

    So far, little has helped. Twitter user growth was flat at the end of 2015, compared to the third quarter of the year, at 320 million monthly visitors.

    “We wanted to focus on what we do best,” Noto said, explaining the sports push. For Twitter, he added, that focus was “live” content.

    The league experimented with live-streaming last season when Yahoo paid about $20 million for the rights to show a Buffalo Bills-Jacksonville Jaguars game played in London in October. In a sop to its broadcast partners and fans, the Bills-Jaguars game was still shown on television in the Buffalo and Jacksonville markets.

    But streaming games free to the more than 180 countries where Twitter is available might attract fans in places where the N.F.L. does not have major broadcast partners.

    Both sides deemed the Yahoo trial a success, which prompted the league to expand its offerings. Yahoo and the league said the game had attracted more than 15.2 million unique users, nearly five times more than the previous record for a streaming audience, the 2014 World Cup match between Belgium and the American national team.

    But according to one mathematical conversion that Yahoo disputes, the 15.2 million unique users for the Bills-Jaguars game amounts to the television equivalent of 2.36 million average viewers, about 1.6 million of whom were in the United States.

    Still, Twitter hopes that the magnetism of the N.F.L. provides them with a sorely needed boost.

    “This to me is much more about publicity and marketing for Twitter than selling ads,” said Richard Greenfield, a media analyst at BTIG Research.
