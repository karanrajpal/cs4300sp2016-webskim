    Yankees’ Brian Cashman and Joe Girardi Are Opposites Who Agree

    By BILLY WITZAPRIL 3, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    When the Yankees played at Toronto in August, their general manager, Brian Cashman, took a trip up the CN Tower, which rises like a giraffe’s neck above the downtown skyline. His purpose: to lean out over a ledge, 1,168 feet above terra firma, tethered only by a cable and a sense of adventure.

    A few hours later, the adrenaline rush still in full force, Cashman bounced around the visitors’ dugout and clubhouse, eagerly sharing photos that captured the sheer terror of his experience.

    Some were envious. Yankees Manager Joe Girardi was incredulous.

    “Why would you do that?” Girardi said, his eyes narrowing, creases forming on his forehead, as if his teenage daughter had come home with a nose ring.

    Cashman is willing to rappel down a 22-story building for charity each December and to jump out of airplanes (something he broke his ankle doing in 2013). Girardi is not.

    “Yeah, no chance,” Girardi said. “I look at some of the things he’s done, like jumping out of a plane, and I’m thinking, ‘What’s wrong with you?’ He’s a risk seeker, and I am not.”

    Girardi and Cashman each are many things the other is not.

    They are beginning their ninth season together, trailing only Bruce Bochy, hired as San Francisco’s manager in October 2006, and Brian Sabean, the Giants’ top baseball decision maker, among baseball’s longest-tenured tandems. But Girardi and Cashman are something of an odd couple.

    It is not just what gets their juices flowing. (Girardi is no daredevil, but he is a fitness freak, often donning weight-lifting belts and gloves to work out in the Yankees’ weight room.) The dichotomy between them may as well begin with where they grew up: Girardi in Peoria, Ill., Cashman just outside New York.

    Girardi is the square-jawed, tight-lipped, former mechanical engineering student whose favorite book is the Bible and who considers himself, with some pride, a doting husband. If there is something surprising, and somewhat subversive, about Girardi, it is that his favorite band is the Rolling Stones.

    Cashman’s lips are far looser, and although he is easygoing, he has a direct manner — he once suggested, using an expletive, that Alex Rodriguez shut up, and he publicly stood up to Derek Jeter’s contract demands. He endured a messy, public divorce, and his reading tastes toggle between biographies of titans of sports and industry and paperback thrillers, the latest being “Flesh and Blood,” by Patricia Cornwell. His guilty listening pleasure, he said, is MLB Radio.

    The common ground shared by Girardi and Cashman is a small parcel — a taste for Italian food and their jobs.

    While the Yankees have not won a playoff game since 2012 — and have not won the World Series since 2009 — Cashman and Girardi have managed an admirable degree of consistency. The team’s .570 winning percentage since the start of the 2009 season is the best in baseball, and the Yankees have remained competitive as they have transitioned to life after Jeter and other big stars — albeit with the help of a payroll in the $200 million range.

    In all sports, there is inherent tension in the relationship between the manager and the general manager, and if a team does not win, its general manager can point the finger at the manager: His decision making is poor. The manager can point the finger at the general manager, too: The players are not good enough.

    In baseball, that dynamic has changed considerably in the last decade as front offices, largely through the use of analytics, have exerted greater influence on lineups, bullpen use and other strategies — areas that were once solely the manager’s realm.

    For the Yankees, the conflict may be further reduced because Cashman’s and Girardi’s contracts are essentially tied together — both are set to expire after the 2017 season. Cashman, 48, and Girardi, 51, also cite two pillars in their relationship: candid dialogue (so that problems do not fester) and a willingness to embrace new ideas.

    “I think he knows that he can speak his mind in a constructive way with me, and I can speak my mind in a constructive way with him,” said Cashman, who speaks with Girardi daily during the season. “There’s an openness that is encouraged here. I acquire the talent, and Joe deploys the talent, and sometimes you’ll have disagreements on players’ capabilities that I’ve acquired or strategies that he’s employing. You’ve got to be able to work through those in a healthy manner to have a successful manager-general-manager relationship, and I’m proud to say we have that.”

    Girardi said his relationship with Cashman provided “a lot” of comfort in 2013 when his previous contract was expiring and the Yankees missed the playoffs. An agreement on a new deal was reached almost immediately after the season.

    Last week, when it came time to choose the final spot in the pitching rotation, 16 people — coaches, executives, scouts and analysts — convened to discuss the merits of C. C. Sabathia and Ivan Nova. Ultimately, Cashman left the decision up to Girardi, who chose Sabathia.

    But after last season, it was Cashman who drove the decision to replace the bullpen coach Gary Tuck and the hitting coach Jeff Pentland, well-regarded coaches who had significant influence on Girardi during his playing career.

    “Those are hard because you have relationships, but you talk about it,” said Girardi, who conceded that he might have dug in his heels if his relationship with Cashman had been different. “The decision was to let them go. You have to let it go and develop a relationship with new people. Even though you might have a lot of faith and trust in those people, you’ve got to move on.”

    After the 2007 season, with the owner George Steinbrenner in decline, it was largely Cashman’s decision whom to hire to replace Manager Joe Torre, who had effectively been forced out. Cashman had observed Girardi while Girardi was playing for the Yankees in the 1990s, had gotten to know him better while Girardi was a color analyst for the YES Network and had followed Girardi’s one year managing the Marlins, in 2006. That season, Girardi was named the National League’s manager of the year, but he was fired after clashing with the owner Jeffrey Loria.

    Cashman, who had assumed his post in 1998, liked Girardi’s leadership qualities, character and baseball mind. He called Girardi a grizzly bear in public and a teddy bear behind closed doors.

    “There’s always a leap of faith and trust that I believe I know what I’m getting,” Cashman said. “But you never know until it plays out.”

    Despite finishing 89-73, the Yankees had a rocky first season under Girardi. Not only did they fail to make the playoffs for the first time in 14 seasons, but Girardi was criticized for being secretive and at times deceitful with the news media, qualities that put him in sharp relief with Torre, whom Cashman referred to as “the Henry Clay of managers, the great communicator.”

    “This Joe is different than that Joe,” Cashman said. “This Joe would rather not deal with the press at all and deal with his players. This Joe’s strength is managing games and players. I think he’s gotten so much better with the press, but I don’t think it’s an area he enjoys.”

    Girardi’s pregame and postgame sessions with the news media sometimes carry the air of a cross-examination. A year ago, he declined to elaborate on why he liked the pairing of Jacoby Ellsbury and Brett Gardner at the top of the lineup because he did not want to give information to the opponent. That was before the Yankees’ first spring training game.

    “People sometimes don’t understand where I come from,” Girardi said. “My job is to win games, so I don’t ever want to give the other team one ounce of an advantage. I will protect players to the nth degree because I remember how difficult it was to play at this level and I believe leadership is based on love and love always protects. So that probably makes me a boring story most of the time.”

    Girardi paused to laugh.

    “That’s my objective,” he said. “I know I’m a boring story.”

    Cashman, since Torre’s departure and Steinbrenner’s 2010 death, has become a more prominent voice for the Yankees. He has also become comfortable speaking his mind. When he traded for closer Aroldis Chapman, who was being investigated by Major League Baseball over domestic violence accusations, Cashman acknowledged an unpopular truth — that he made the trade because the Yankees could get Chapman cheap.

    “I know how the game gets played,” Cashman said of his interactions with the news media. “But I would say it’s easier to land the plane than it is to continue rerouting around the airport and wasting more time trying to find a new approach to land the plane. I’d rather just land the plane, and many times being brutally honest about a circumstance is better for the writers, it’s better for the fan base, and it’s better for me.”

    Speaking one’s mind, of course, is no more dangerous than hanging off tall buildings, jumping out of airplanes or riding a zip line through a Costa Rican jungle. And if interacting with the New York news media is portrayed as swimming with sharks, well, Cashman has done that, too — while scuba diving.

    He does not have a death wish, just a zest for life.

    “I don’t call it adrenaline,” Cashman said. “I just call it living.”

    Girardi would call it something else.

    This is just how it is for Girardi and Cashman, with their disparate backgrounds, personalities and viewpoints and their shared goal: to win another World Series, one they can celebrate together — in their own ways.
