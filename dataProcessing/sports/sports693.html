    After Spurt, Holy Cross Finally Lives Down to Expectations

    By TIM ROHANMARCH 18, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    SPOKANE, Wash. — Over the last three weeks or so, as Holy Cross made its improbable run to the N.C.A.A. tournament, Coach Bill Carmody took a book with him to read on the long bus and plane rides home after games: “The Lives of the Artists,” one of the foremost pieces of literature on art history, written by the Italian artist Giorgio Vasari in the 16th century.

    The book details the development of Renaissance art through biographical accounts of several artists, including Donatello, Leonardo and Michelangelo. Carmody kept the book in tow partly out of superstition — the Crusaders kept winning — but also for inspiration.

    “I like reading it because they thought differently,” Carmody said Thursday. “All these guys did things that hadn’t happened before. They built this duomo in Florence, and just the architecture behind it, they had never thought: ‘Oh, you can do it that way? Whoa.’ ”

    That kind of thinking served Holy Cross well because its run defied convention. At the start of March, the Crusaders entered the Patriot League tournament as a No. 9 seed, with a 10-19 record, having not won a conference road game. They proceeded to win four road games to secure the conference’s only tournament berth and edged Southern in a play-in game on a neutral court Wednesday to send them to a matchup against No. 1-seeded Oregon on Friday night.

    Finally, the Crusaders looked the way they had for much of the season. The Ducks (29-6) steamrollered them, 91-52, recording the tournament’s largest margin of victory to that point.

    For about 40 hours, though, Carmody and his team had enjoyed being the tournament’s biggest surprise, with dreams that they could become the first 16th seed to defeat a No. 1 seed.

    To most fans, the most recognizable thing about Holy Cross (15-20) was its coach. Carmody had spent 13 years at Northwestern without making the N.C.A.A. tournament, drawing publicity because Northwestern was (and remains) the only university in a Power 5 conference to have never made the tournament. The pressure mounted each year until the university fired him in 2013.

    “I don’t feel like talking about it,” Carmody said.

    Carmody took two years off and had no plans to return to coaching when Holy Cross called last year. It was one of the Division I jobs that would prove tougher than Northwestern.

    At the time, the Crusaders had not won a tournament game since 1953. The program’s best players, Bob Cousy — who, as a freshman in 1947, helped Holy Cross secure its only title — and Tom Heinsohn, were in their 80s. After Carmody agreed to become the coach, he found that the gym had not changed since he had last been there — in the mid ’70s.

    The team needed just as much fixing up. Entering the conference tournament, Holy Cross had lost 13 of its last 17 games, including its last five, and its Princeton offense barely resembled the version Carmody had learned as a longtime assistant under the storied coach Pete Carril.

    Around that time, Carmody made two drastic changes. He began starting Eric Green — a senior who returned midseason from an anterior cruciate ligament injury — and bringing Robert Champion off the bench as a spark plug. And with Green roaming the court at full strength, Carmody went almost exclusively to a 1-3-1 defense, which hid the Crusaders’ poor lateral quickness.

    “As we started to trust each other, that zone got a lot better,” Holy Cross Athletic Director Nathan Pine said. “All of a sudden, some of our back cuts started clicking.”

    That recipe worked against Bucknell, Army and Lehigh — but against Oregon?

    Dealing with the news media, Carmody tried diffusing the pressure by being self-deprecating. He joked that he would not hold two practices Thursday, to avoid using up his players’ “quota of shots for the month,” and that if they won out, they would finish the season with a winning record.

    But privately, Carmody was obsessing over his game plan. Do not rush for offensive rebounds. Get back on defense. Take good shots. And how can you slow them defensively?

    Carmody has compiled bits and pieces from his experience in high-stakes games over the years. During his time as a Princeton assistant, the Tigers nearly upset No. 1-seeded Georgetown in 1989, losing on a noncall on their final shot, and they knocked off fourth-seeded U.C.L.A. in 1996. The next day, The Daily Princetonian’s headline read, “David 43, Goliath 41.”

    Carmody sent his current team a Sports Illustrated article describing that 1989 Princeton-Georgetown game in detail and held it as an example that smaller programs needed to be in the tournament. “I wanted to put it in perspective,” he said, “that things like this happen.”

    When Carmody woke up Thursday after a long night of travel, on the eve of his next chance to make tournament history, he went down the street from the team hotel and picked up a purple dress shirt from a men’s clothing store. He had packed only one suit for the trip to the play-in game, not wanting to disturb the team’s good vibes.

    “I brought one suit, but I wasn’t packing two,” Carmody said. “I’m a firm believer in the whammy, you know?”

    It became apparent early in the Oregon game, though, that his superstitions would not matter. The Ducks eviscerated the 1-3-1 defense, snaking their way around it, bullying their way through it or lobbing alley-oops over it. Oregon often beat Holy Cross down the court before the defense could be set up, or simply volleyed the ball off the backboard until it went in.

    Carmody paced the sideline with his hands on his hips, shouting directives. At halftime, he had so much work to do, with the Crusaders trailing by 18, that he took off his jacket as he left the court. In the second half, as Oregon piled on, Carmody sat on the bench and rested his head on his hand.

    Afterward, he spoke glumly.

    “The Patriot League,” he said, “doesn’t have guys like that.”
