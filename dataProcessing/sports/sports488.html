    Knicks Show Some Life, but Focus Is on Team’s Next Incarnation

    Sports of The Times

    By MICHAEL POWELL MARCH 25, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Carmelo Anthony isolates on Derrick Rose, ready to do the bump-and-shimmy that is his career-long groove. Another Chicago player drops off to help defend him, and a stork-thin Latvian cuts beyond the top of the key.

    Melo flips a sweet little bounce pass, and that Latvian stork, who happens to be the multitalented future of this Knicks franchise, catches it and launches a soft beauty of a 3-point shot.

    This was a scene on Thursday night from an improbable domination by the Knicks, who took two games in a row from the playoff-contending Bulls. These are not reasonable facsimiles of the Bulls of recent vintage. Management last season forced out Tom Thibodeau, one of the true defensive coaching geniuses in the N.B.A., and the team has not looked the same since.

    As that malpractice for once belongs to the Bulls and not to the Knicks, we will set it aside for now.

    These Knicks are nothing if not confounding. In the first few months of the season, they appeared to have a workable mix of young and old, an aging star and a sterling rookie, Kristaps Porzingis. Then they fell off a cliff, losing games in great batches.

    They went to this franchise’s go-to move and fired their coach. The Knicks have had 10 coaches in the last 12 years, which more or less precludes coherence.

    Their loss at home Sunday to the reliably putrid Sacramento Kings featured their worst 2016 traits: listless defense, shooting that more properly looked like flinging, passing to men who weren’t cutting, and cutting to men who weren’t passing.

    Madison Square Garden was a mausoleum. I felt as if I’d resurfaced in 1985. Where was Ken (the Animal) Bannister? “It was not a stellar basketball game,” George Karl, the Kings’ no-doubt-doomed coach, said afterward in an outburst of honesty.

    So I came to this set against the Bulls prepared not to praise but to bury, my collapsible spade tucked in my backpack. And good God if the Knicks didn’t play pleasurable hoops.

    These games put into relief two difficult decisions the Knicks must make at season’s end: What becomes of Carmelo Anthony, and who will coach this group?

    Let’s deal with the former first.

    I have not been among the pack-up-Anthony-and-mail-him-off-C.O.D. crowd. His scoring has dropped considerably this season, and that’s for the better. That probably reflects recognition of the toll taken by injury and mortality, and perhaps a buy-in to the team president Phil Jackson’s philosophy. The remainder of his game has rarely been better. He is pulling down about eight rebounds per game and, for the first time in his career, dealing out more than four assists a game.

    Anthony understands he is a leader now, and even appears to care about defense now and then.

    Porzingis credits Anthony with helping him come to understand the intricacies of post play. When he perfects the turn and square, the jab step, the spin, this balletic 7-foot-3 fellow will become even more dangerous.

    But a low-30s-win team that possesses a 31-year-old star with creaky knees is a bit like a poor man who scrapes for food even as he owns a beautiful old Porsche. What is the cost-benefit analysis? (Coach Kurt Rambis is playing Anthony, bad knee and all, a risky 37 minutes per game, three or four minutes longer than most league stars.)

    Anthony has a no-trade contract clause, and his desire to remain in New York City is admirable. I dig this city, too, and I’d just as soon The New York Times not trade me to Minnesota. Unless the Knicks win an improbable free-agent sweepstakes and Kevin Durant suits up for them next season, they will have a young and developing star, a couple of serviceable starters like Robin Lopez and Langston Galloway, and this aging star.

    Perhaps they could cut a deal with a team like the Boston Celtics, loaded to the gills with young talent and first-round draft picks?

    Then there is the question of the coach. Jackson, the resident swami, suggested recently that he might retain Rambis, the interim coach. That led to a spate of articles that suggested Rambis was to coaching what George III was to North American colonies: a man with no feel for his work. Critics note Rambis’s career coaching record, which is a homely 63-157.

    There’s an unfair aspect to this critique. Rambis served as coaching aide-de-camp to Jackson on those great Lakers teams, and in a brief toe-dip as the coach of that team he compiled a winning record and won a playoff round.

    Then he was sentenced to coaching the Minnesota Timberwolves, one of the league’s worst-managed teams. He had an aging star, journeymen, point guards who couldn’t pass and a young Kevin Love, who rebounded fiercely and played defense with even less of a clue than he does today.

    There is a freshly written bill of indictment of Rambis with the Knicks. He stands accused of asking Porzingis to play inside, a sin, it is argued, that shows he fails to comprehend the 3-point aerial bombardment ways of the modern N.B.A. Chris Herring of The Wall Street Journal is a thoughtful and careful analyst, and he has pointed out that Porzingis has received the ball in the post almost twice as often under Rambis as under the former coach Derek Fisher. And Porzingis has faltered down low.

    Yet the Latvian has proved himself an omnivorous learner all season. Early on, he picked up fouls by the minute and could not stay on the floor. He adjusted, and that doesn’t happen much anymore.

    Against the Bulls, he looked as if he might be picking up a new groove. He dived to the hoop with abandon, and turned and shot in the face of a fine defender in Taj Gibson.

    Continue reading the main story

    That created space for him at the 3-point line, where this lean pterodactyl of a player is deadly. In the last two games, he shot 18 for 31 for 48 points, and collected 20 rebounds and blocked three shots.

    “It’s a learning experience,” he said. “I always see my weaknesses; my strengths come naturally.”

    Perhaps Coach Rambis, whose style is West Coast relaxed, is not a preposterous thought for the future. All of this said, Jackson can be mystifying in his stubbornness.

    He seeks devotees of his cherished triangle, but it’s not clear how he could justify not calling Thibodeau and having a long conversation about the coaching vacancy.

    Then there is David Blatt, a congenitally creative international coach who rode the Cleveland Cavaliers to the N.B.A. finals in his rookie season. He was tossed aside this year, LeBron roadkill. It is not so difficult to envision his cut-and-flow offense working in New York.

    The Knicks are likely to end this season with twice as many wins as last year. The good news ends there. They won only 17 games last season.

    The climb ahead is up a cliff wall with teams like the Warriors and the Spurs and the Cavaliers having a moondance at the top. This Knicks team will not make that climb.
