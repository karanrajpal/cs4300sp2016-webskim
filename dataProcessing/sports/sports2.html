    Real Madrid’s Emergency Strategy: Leave It to Cristiano Ronaldo

    By ROB HUGHESAPRIL 13, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    LONDON — The word superstar is too loosely used today in regard to team games, but it is impossible to imagine Real Madrid getting out of the hole it had dug for itself in the Champions League without its brightest talent, Cristiano Ronaldo. After losing the first leg in Germany, 2-0, Real Madrid had to beat Wolfsburg by three clear goals Tuesday at Santiago Bernabéu stadium in Madrid. Ronaldo, looking like he had ESP, scored all three goals in Real’s 3-0 victory.

    Madrid is a flawed team that, like many other Real teams dating to the 1950s, relies upon exceptional individuals to win on any given night. Ronaldo is the latest continuation of that. Real needed three goals, and Ronaldo scored them.

    Karim Benzema and Gareth Bale, superb players in their own right who cost more than $170 million combined to acquire, were the supporting cast. They played O.K. within the team context, but neither scored. None of Ronaldo’s goals were anywhere close to a masterpiece. The first two were a matter of being in the right place at the right time, of sensing an opportunity and then reacting to a lack of concentration by the Wolfsburg defense. (The first goal also involved a lucky break, a ricochet off a defender’s shin.)

    But there he was, Johnny on the spot.

    The decisive third goal was a free kick, but again, it was not a trademark Ronaldo strike of awesome beauty or power or timing. Ronaldo said that Real’s goalkeeper, Keylor Navas, had spoken to him just before the free kick and suggested he surprise everyone by shooting it softly for once.

    Ronaldo did just that, and, uncannily, two players in the defensive wall — Naldo and Josuha Guilavogui — parted just as the ball reached them, leaving a hole that deceived their own goalkeeper.

    “It’s mythical, magical,” Sergio Ramos, the Madrid captain, told reporters after the game. “Cris is always important, he’s our goal scorer, our icon.

    “We don’t need to flatter him any more,” Ramos concluded. “He’s a man who gives everything for the badge, and when he’s on form, the team knows it.”

    That in itself defines the culture at Real Madrid. The current coach, Zinedine Zidane, was once able to conjure up majestic game-winning shots when he was a player, including his finest goal of all, which won the 2002 Champions League final in Glasgow.

    That, too, was against a German opponent, Bayer Leverkusen. It was an imperious goal, a volley struck at hip height with the flawless technique of a great player.

    The instinct that made him a great player may or may not make Zidane a great coach for Madrid, if he is given enough time. But can someone who has been there and done that in a similar situation be credited with knowing that the best plan Tuesday was to leave it all to Ronaldo?

    Ronaldo is 31, an age when the body usually begins to slow, but the numbers indicate that the Portuguese star is anything but waning. In the Champions League alone this season — because it is so intrinsic to Real Madrid’s history — Ronaldo has scored 16 times in 10 games. On Tuesday, he scored his third hat trick in the tournament this season as he increased his career tally in the event to 93 goals. In 129 Champions League games for Manchester United and for Real, Ronaldo has scored 63 goals with his right foot, 14 with his left foot and 16 with his head. His goal off the free kick was the 11th time he has done that in the competition.

    Multiply all of that many times over and you have Ronaldo’s career total of 482 goals in 665 games, with 359 goals coming in 342 appearances for Real Madrid.

    Ronaldo repeated on Tuesday a mantra that he has been fond of lately: The stats, he said, do not lie. “Una noche perfecta,” or one perfect night, said Ramos, looking across at Ronaldo, who had the game ball tucked inside the jersey like he was pregnant.

    All of this tells us nothing about Real Madrid’s strange season or its ability to advance and perhaps win an unprecedented 11th title in Europe’s top club competition.

    Like the Ronaldo who came before him (the Brazilian who played in Madrid from 2002 to 2007), Cristiano is a phenomenon. But how does a coach, particularly an inexperienced one like Zidane, build a reliable team plan around one player’s extrasensory perception?

    The plan worked handsomely on Tuesday. Ronaldo worked it, but the effort was not entirely solo because the team’s defense, more compact in shape, was far more reliable than it was in Wolfsburg.

    Yet there is a thought around the Bernabéu that this summer will be the time to offload Ronaldo. To sell him while his fame might still fetch a decent price. To reconstruct the team, to maybe liberate talents of its other stars, such as Bale.

    It is all speculation. Nobody knows until Friday which opponent Madrid will face in the semifinals. And no one can say if Madrid’s philosophy of acquiring star after star and throwing a bunch of them at a coach — any coach — can work in the modern game.

    It failed in Wolfsburg last week, but it worked out quite well in Madrid on Tuesday.

    All of us, if we are honest, have no idea what makes Ronaldo such a singularly extraordinary winner. His talents are many, and his attitude at times is that of a prima donna. But he delivers, with quite frightening regularity, the ability to destroy two game plans — both his team’s and the opponent’s.
