    Breanna Stewart Is a Syracuse Fan but a UConn Star

    By SETH BERKMANAPRIL 4, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    INDIANAPOLIS — Few players on the Syracuse women’s basketball team have as long a history with the program as Breanna Stewart does.

    Although she plays for the Connecticut Huskies, Stewart has a collection of Orange apparel. From her home in North Syracuse, the Syracuse campus is a five-minute drive south on Interstate 81, a trip she regularly made to watch women’s basketball games with her father, Brian, when she was a high school student. When Stewart is on break from Connecticut, she sometimes travels that route to visit Brittney Sykes, a Syracuse guard, whom she has known since ninth grade.

    With her relationship to the university in mind, Stewart said it was fitting that she would end her college career against Syracuse on Tuesday night in the national championship game at Bankers Life Fieldhouse.

    “I’ve been around that program for a while,” said Stewart, who attended basketball camps at Syracuse, where she cultivated bonds with the men’s basketball coach, Jim Boeheim, and the family of the women’s basketball coach, Quentin Hillsman. “Growing up with a lot of people whose parents are in the athletic department, there’s a lot of relationships there.”

    In 2011, when Stewart was one of the nation’s most highly recruited players, Syracuse’s accolades were in short supply. In its four previous N.C.A.A. women’s tournament appearances, it had not won a game.

    UConn had accrued seven national titles, and has added three more during Stewart’s career.

    “Stewie is a huge Syracuse fan,” Connecticut Coach Geno Auriemma said, referring to Stewart by her nickname. “She knows. And she’s come right out and said it. Like, if she was a guy playing high school ball there, there’s no doubt in her mind she’d be playing at Syracuse. But it just wasn’t the right time for her to go there, and Connecticut was a better place for her.”

    Stewart said that the Orange made her top 10 list of potential colleges, but that UConn’s reputation for molding players and her desire to become one of the sport’s greatest players led her out of upstate New York.

    On Monday, Stewart said that she was proud of Syracuse’s run in the tournament this season.

    “I was happy to see Syracuse win,” she said of the Orange’s 80-59 victory over Washington on Sunday in the national semifinals. “I’m just really happy that they’ve gotten this far. It’s great for them.”

    Stewart added, “Anytime they’re on, I’ll watch them, because I have the relationships with the players that I have.”

    Stewart has known Sykes the longest, dating to when they were teammates on the Philadelphia Belles Amateur Athletic Union team.

    During high school, they discussed playing together in college, but, as Sykes said, “She wanted to be coached by Geno and I wanted to be coached by Quentin.”

    Sykes added: “I wanted to go to a program where I could help and build that program and make history.”

    Before this season, Syracuse had never advanced past the tournament’s second round. But as the No. 4 seed in the Sioux Falls Region, the Orange, with their defensive press, defeated top-seeded South Carolina and seventh-seeded Tennessee, landing them in Indianapolis and eventually in a showdown against the undefeated Huskies (37-0), who are looking to win their fourth consecutive Division I title.

    For Sykes, the journey has been redeeming. In the 2014 tournament, Hillsman held her head as she lay on the floor after tearing her right anterior cruciate ligament in a first-round game. Ten months later, she tore the same A.C.L. on Jan. 4, 2015, in a game against Notre Dame.

    During much of her rehab, she blocked calls from a majority of her social circle. One of the few people she would talk to was Stewart, who on Monday called Sykes her best friend.

    “She knew how to talk to me; she knew what to say, what not to say,” Sykes said.

    Stewart’s family sent Sykes care packages of books and candy. They called her and offered their home if she needed time to get away.

    “Her parents are like my parents away from home,” said Sykes, who often attends recreational baseball and basketball games involving Stewart’s younger brother. “They made sure they checked on me. I really appreciated that from her and her family. It’s a lot of love in that family, and that’s what you need going through something like that.”

    Stewart said, “My relationship with Brittney Sykes is, I would say, like no other.”

    Stewart’s sympathy for another player also resonated Sunday when her freshman teammate Katie Lou Samuelson, a starting forward, broke a bone in her left foot.

    On the first possession of UConn’s 80-51 win over Oregon State, Samuelson drove for a layup and felt a pop in her foot. Samuelson, who had never had a broken bone, thought a knuckle had popped.

    She played through the pain but returned to the bench early in the third quarter wearing a black walking boot.

    When Stewart was removed from the game in the fourth quarter, she consoled Samuelson, telling her, “We got this.”

    Auriemma said Samuelson’s length — she stands 6 feet 3 — would have been useful against Syracuse’s backcourt. There were whispers that she was ready to inherit Stewart’s role as UConn’s next great swing player. But even without her talent, the Huskies barely missed a step against Oregon State.

    After the game, Auriemma told Samuelson that he expected her to work even harder to return and put her own mark on the Final Four, “but right now, it’s not your time: It’s somebody else’s time.”
