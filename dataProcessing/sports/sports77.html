    Exaggerator Carries Brothers to Kentucky Derby

    By MIKE TIERNEYAPRIL 9, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    ARCADIA, Calif. — The trainer-jockey dynamic in horse racing can be volatile enough without the added variable of common parentage.

    Keith and Kent Desormeaux are headstrong and, as is typical between siblings, often blunt with each other. A partnership in this sport would seem fraught with risks.

    But they are of one mind with Exaggerator, the 3-year-old trained by Keith and ridden by Kent.

    A close third choice among bettors and a distant third in the buildup in the news media, Exaggerator skimmed across a sloppy track to take the Santa Anita Derby on Saturday. The result positions the Desormeauxs to become only the third brother tandem in the 142-year history of the Kentucky Derby on May 7.

    While Keith, 49, is older by three years and owns an impressive résumé, Kent has three Kentucky Derby wins and Hall of Fame recognition. Neither maintains a clear-cut upper hand.

    “I would say we have a very frank relationship,” Keith said after visiting the Santa Anita Park winner’s circle. “If I feel a certain way about something, I don’t hold anything back, and the same for him.”

    When two days of rain caused the course to come up sloppy for only the fifth time in 79 Derbys, the pair pondered whether to put their customary closer nearer to the leader during the race. Making up ground on so-called off tracks is often more difficult than on a dry surface.

    They chose not to rewrite the playbook. In the paddock, the trainer confided to the jockey, “Kent, I’m telling you that I’ve never seen this horse so confident.”

    Keith remained mystified at Exaggerator’s third place a month ago behind Mor Spirit and Danzing Candy, the public’s one-two picks Saturday. He did not share his horse’s confidence.

    “Did I have doubts as they were going up to the gate?” Keith said. “No doubt I had doubts.”

    But once on the track, Kent noticed that the surface would be no hindrance.

    “He was the fourth horse that I rode on the mud today, and the others felt like they had ice skates on,” Kent said. “Exaggerator felt like he had track shoes on.”

    The footing was just as dicey in the Wood Memorial, as the drumroll leading up to the Kentucky Derby quickened Saturday with three vital prep races.

    At Aqueduct, a timely head bob barely got Outwork to the wire ahead of Trojan Nation, whose 81-to-1 odds would have qualified him as one of most improbable Wood winners. Trojan Nation’s previous best was third in a maiden event.

    Outwork, the second betting choice behind Shagaf on a soupy course, assures the trainer Todd Pletcher of a role in the Kentucky Derby, where he is a fixture. Outwork needed the points from the Derby qualifying formula. Shagaf had banked enough points beforehand as necessary insurance for what became a fifth-place finish.

    The Bluegrass Stakes in Lexington, Ky., was blessed with a fast course, and the second wagering preference also took it.

    Brody’s Cause (4-to-1), readied by Dale Romans, rallied from the back of the pack through a crowded field of 14 to edge My Man Sam. Pletcher’s joy from the Wood was tempered when the favored Zulu, one of his three Bluegrass entrants, failed to threaten and trudged in 12th.

    At Santa Anita, the jockey Mike Smith gunned Danzing Candy to a considerable lead, but Exaggerator swept past him early in the stretch to breeze by six and a half lengths. Of the three primary contenders, only Exaggerator had won on an off track.

    As impressive as he was, Exaggerator will yield favorite’s status at the Derby to Nyquist. He has yet to defeat the Florida Derby champion in three attempts.

    But anticipating his first try at the Derby, Keith intends to enjoy the figurative ride while his younger brother enjoys the actual one.

    “For Exaggerator, the pedigree is there,” Keith said. “It’s going to what be what his attitude will be leaving the gate in front of 150,000 fans — and a nervous trainer.”

    There is not likely to be any head-butting over strategy despite the siblings’ vast difference in Derby experience.

    Kent has braced for the possibility of shedding tears in the Derby post parade as he thinks of his older brother’s rockier journey.

    “It’s extra special to be with Keith, and we don’t talk about it too much,” Kent said. “It’s all quite understood.”

    The undercard at Santa Anita involved a filly that is widely regarded as the most talented 3-year-old.

    Hardly breaking a sweat, the unbeaten Songbird coasted to her seventh win at rock-bottom odds of 1 to 9 in the Santa Anita Oaks. Songbird’s camp has clung to a gender-specific path and intends to skip the Kentucky Derby.

    Bob Baffert, who claimed his fourth Derby last year with American Pharoah, can double his chances with Mor Spirit and Cupid, the probable favorite in the Arkansas Derby next Saturday.
