    N.C.A.A. Tournament: Middle Tennessee State Sets Tone on a Day of Upsets

    By ZACH SCHONBRUNMARCH 18, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    The odds looked strong for Michigan State — not just to make it past the first weekend of the N.C.A.A. men’s basketball tournament, but to roll on to the Final Four, the coveted perch Coach Tom Izzo has practically transformed into his home away from home.

    But in 40 minutes Friday afternoon, those notions — along with millions of brackets — came crashing to the floor.

    Middle Tennessee State, the No. 15 seed in the Midwest Region, downed the mighty Spartans — the Big Ten tournament champions and strong title contenders — in one of the most stunning first-round upsets in N.C.A.A. tournament history, swiping the spotlight on a day filled with unpredictability.

    After opening with a whimper, the tournament returned with a roar Friday afternoon, and no favorite seemed safe. Two No. 10 seeds, Syracuse and Virginia Commonwealth, upended their seventh-seeded opponents in convincing fashion.

    And California, playing as a No. 4 seed in the South, the highest seeding in the program’s history, fell to 13th-seeded Hawaii, 77-66, giving the Rainbow Warriors their first N.C.A.A. tournament win in five tries.

    The turmoil for the top seeds continued late into the night. No. 14-seeded Stephen F. Austin maintained the longest winning streak in the nation (21 games) with an upset of West Virginia, 70-56, in Brooklyn. The Lumberjacks, led by guard Thomas Walkup, who fittingly has not shaved since November, forced 22 Mountaineers turnovers. West Virginia finished the game 16 for its last 52 from the field.

    “If you wrote down how to lose a game, you could probably check off just about everything that you wrote down,” West Virginia Coach Bob Huggins told reporters. “You could probably make a list and check it off and say, ‘Yeah, they did all those things.’ ”

    Never before had teams seeded 13th, 14th and 15th won games in the N.C.A.A. tournament on the same day.

    A second No. 15 seed, Weber State, gave Xavier a momentary scare, trailing by only 7 points in the second half, but the Musketeers held on, 71-53. Notre Dame, a No. 6 seed, withstood a thrilling seesaw battle with Michigan to win, 70-63.

    But the game of the evening — and the shot of the tournament — arrived courtesy of Northern Iowa and Texas, which battled into the late hours in Oklahoma City.

    What else did this wild day need? How about a halfcourt buzzer-beater?

    O.K., then. Paul Jesperson’s heave as time expired gave the Panthers a 75-72 upset of No. 6-seeded Texas. Because of course.

    The score had just been tied with three seconds remaining by Texas’ Isaiah Taylor, whose jumper appeared to send the game to overtime. But Northern Iowa scrambled to get the ball in — declining to use its last timeout — and Jesperson found the ball in his hands.

    He banked in the game-winning 3-pointer from just beyond halfcourt.

    Thus ended a fantastic game that featured multiple lead changes in the final minutes. Northern Iowa had presented a matchup problem for Texas throughout the night. The Panthers, who do not have a starter taller than 6 feet 9 inches, like to spread the floor, work the shot clock and open up driving lanes to the hoop. Northern Iowa led by as many as 16 in the first half after going on a 23-2 run.

    Texas responded by opening the second half on a 17-3 run. Taylor (22 points) got hot, and the Longhorns regained a lead as large as 5. But the Panthers, no strangers to N.C.A.A. tournament success, kept the score tight.

    Still, nothing could prepare college basketball fans for the stunner in St. Louis. It seemed so incomprehensible that Middle Tennessee State could win the game that 99.1 percent of the participants in Yahoo Sports pools picked Michigan State to advance. By game’s end, only six perfect brackets remained in ESPN’s tournament challenge, out of 13 million entries.

    In fact, Michigan State was the second most popular pick to win the national title this year, behind top-seeded Kansas, according to Yahoo. The Spartans had not lost since Feb. 9, against Purdue, and led by Denzel Valentine, their national player of the year candidate, there was good reason to believe they might not again this season.

    Middle Tennessee State, on the other hand, finished second in Conference USA but won its tournament title, earning only its second N.C.A.A. tournament berth in 27 years.

    A No. 15 seed had beaten a No. 2 seed only seven times in N.C.A.A tournament history, and these two programs appeared as far apart as any of the teams in the other upsets. Michigan State was favored by 17½ points entering the matchup, and many were already looking forward to the team’s next-round matchup against Syracuse, which beat Dayton, 70-51, earlier on Friday.

    “I’ve had some tough losses in my career,” Izzo said. “But I don’t think they’ll be any tougher than this one.”

    Michigan State never led. The Blue Raiders shot 55.9 percent from the field. Reggie Upshaw finished with 21 points, and all five Middle Tennessee State starters scored in double figures.

    Middle Tennessee State jumped out to a 15-2 lead in the early minutes, shooting 6 for 6 from the field, including 3 for 3 from 3-point range, while the Spartans missed four of their first five shots.

    “We just failed defensively today,” said forward Matt Costello, who led Michigan State with 22 points. “That’s where we’ve hung our hat all year, and we couldn’t stop them.”

    No. 10-seeded Temple almost managed to complete the Friday afternoon sweep of 7-10 upsets, battling Iowa into overtime. But the Hawkeyes won at the buzzer on a tip-in by Adam Woodberry.

    It was that kind of day.
