    As Baseball Arrives, There’s No End in Sight to Cable-Channel Conflicts

    TV Sports

    By RICHARD SANDOMIR APRIL 1, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    As opening day nears, many Yankees and Dodgers fans have reason to feel their loyalties are being tested by parallel corporate disputes.

    They are battles that sports fans should be used to, between regional sports networks and the distributors that carry them. They involve, as they inevitably do, price. And the cost of these channels — which is ultimately borne by customers of cable, satellite TV and other telecommunications companies — is too often eye-popping. Fans, who are not part of any of these negotiations, are squeezed financially without much recourse.

    In the broad sweep of the New York market, 900,000 Comcast customers in New Jersey, Connecticut and Pennsylvania are without the YES Network, the home of the Yankees since 2002. Out West, much of the expansive Los Angeles market has been without the Dodgers’ channel, SportsNet LA, since it was created two years ago.

    The Los Angeles impasse is at a critical stage because Vin Scully has said that this season, his 67th, will be his final season as the nonpareil voice of the Dodgers. Early last season, he stood in the booth at Dodger Stadium and told me that he could not let the shrunken size of his television audience affect how he approached his work. “To me,” he said, “I’m always talking to 100 percent of the audience — and every game is like the World Series.” Barring a settlement, he will be singing about Yasiel Puig and Clayton Kershaw to an audience forcibly pared through no fault of its own.

    Opening day should be viewed as a deadline to make a deal in both markets. But it is likely to be another day of bicoastal gridlock.

    In New York, executives of Comcast and YES (in which 21st Century Fox holds an 80 percent stake) tell nearly irreconcilable stories about their dispute. They can’t even agree on when they held their last negotiation: to Comcast, it was two weeks ago; to YES, it was in November.

    Marcien Jenckes, Comcast’s executive vice president for consumer services, said: “There is a big value gap between the fees that Fox is trying to extract for YES and what it’s worth to our subscribers.” He added that YES was already the most expensive regional sports network.

    YES insisted that throughout 2015, Comcast had agreed to pay a substantial increase to YES (through extensions after their contract had expired) and promised not to pull the channel off its systems. But Comcast then reneged, YES said, and dropped it after the Yankees’ season had ended but early in the Nets’ season. “We paid the higher rate,” said Jenckes, “but that shouldn’t be read as our implicit acceptance of it.”

    Tracy Dolgin, YES’s chief executive, spent several minutes during a telephone interview dissecting the language of a Comcast ad as if he were teaching grammar. Dolgin said of Comcast: “They’re trying to mislead customers that we just asked for a price increase, when we didn’t, and that they’re working to hold down costs for consumers. What they’re really doing is holding down costs for themselves.”

    Asked what he and YES could do to jump-start the talks, he said there wasn’t much to be done. “I can’t turn us back on,” he said. “And it’s not realistic for me to lower my price below what the bigger guys in the market are paying. It would cause chaos.”

    YES is the most expensive regional sports network, according to calculations by SNL Kagan, the media research firm. YES’s subscriber fee of $5.36 a month is above SportsNet LA’s $4.59. But one major factor separates them. YES has two anchor teams: the Yankees (who averaged 259,000 viewers last season) and the Nets (whose audience has been nearly halved, to 44,000, in a dismal season largely without Comcast customers). But the Dodgers are the only denizens of SportsNet LA.

    The Dodgers’ network is worth its fee only as a justification for the daffy $8.35 billion Time Warner Cable is paying to carry the Dodgers on SportsNet LA for 25 years. The only truly compliant taker for the Dodgers’ network has been Time Warner Cable, which is also the dominant cable operator in Los Angeles. Charter became compliant in June only after it had agreed to acquire Time Warner Cable, a transaction that government regulators have not yet approved. Before that, it was a recalcitrant party.

    In the past week or so, there has been the illusion of action in Los Angeles. Time Warner Cable made two offers to the companies you could call the Resistance: DirecTV, AT&T, Cox and Verizon. The first was the Vin Scully discount: a one-year, 30 percent cut to the SportsNet LA fee, which would presumably revert to the higher one after Scully calls his final game, and the fight would ignite anew.

    So far, no one has said yes to the cut-rate “Get Your Scully Now” deal, or to another, longer-term proposal.

    For Dodgers fans deprived of SportsNet LA, there are few options for watching their team, except to become customers of Time Warner Cable, Charter or Bright House Networks (which Charter is also acquiring). Or they can wait for the occasional game on ESPN, Fox or FS1. But they cannot watch any games on a local broadcast station because the Dodgers do not have a deal with one, as they did in the past.

    And while most Yankees games are carried by YES, 21 will be shown on WPIX-TV. But the first one will not be seen until April 30.

    So fans among the 900,000 Comcast subscribers without YES will start their season suffering.

    STUMBLES IN ESPN BROADCAST For many viewers of ESPN on Wednesday night who watched Texas Western beat Kentucky in the 1966 N.C.A.A. men’s basketball championship game, it was a first. It was fascinating to watch as the Miners, who started five African-Americans, defied all expectations to beat the Wildcats.

    But it was not the original broadcast, as ESPN originally claimed and which I wrote about on Monday. It was actually coaches’ film that had been synchronized to a Kentucky radio call. This version was probably familiar to some people because it looked exactly like a DVD of the game that has been available for several years, said Steve Kaplowitz, an ESPN Radio host in El Paso. Kaplowitz said he had helped acquire the audio for the DVD and hosted a show with Don Haskins, the coach of Texas Western (now the University of Texas at El Paso). Haskins died in 2008.

    There were two problems with ESPN’s part of the production. One, it repeatedly posted graphics that misidentified the radio announcer, Claude Sullivan, as Walt Sullivan. ESPN also used a video from President Obama praising Texas Western’s historic achievement without saying that it had been issued in February.
