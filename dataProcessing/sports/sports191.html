    Some Yankees Tickets Require a Long Walk

    By NATE SCHWEBERAPRIL 5, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    After Monday’s rainout, Tuesday’s rescheduled Yankees home opener had the look of perfect baseball weather. But the temperature was in the mid-30s, with a slicing wind that the Arctic would envy. This fact was felt most acutely by the numerous fans who had bought their tickets from StubHub, a website for ticket resales.

    “This is ridiculous, insane — how about just printing it out like you were able to do forever?” said George Magnello, 48, a financial worker who lives in Manhattan, after he endured a frigid walk to redeem his StubHub purchase so he could get into Yankee Stadium.

    Magnello was one of hundreds of Yankees fans who, having made online purchases from StubHub, were forced to walk seven-tenths of a mile south from Yankee Stadium to a StubHub outlet to pick up real tickets. No longer could they simply print their StubHub tickets at home on paper, with nothing more required to pass through the turnstile than the bar code on the printout.

    Instead, a little flustered, and freezing, they plowed through the cold, sponged a little warmth in the StubHub outlet, and then re-emerged with those old-fashioned tickets in their hands and vapor on their breaths. Then they hiked back to the ballpark.

    “It’s a little chilly,” said Thomas Owen, 25, who is from Liverpool, England, but flew in from an oil field in Dubai, in the United Arab Emirates, for his first game.

    Owen was deadpan when he reflected on the ticket walk he had to take south on River Avenue, past noisy bars that smelled of beer and hot dogs, under the clanking No. 4 train tracks, between a gray-concrete parking garage and weathered apartment buildings before he arrived at the StubHub office on the ground floor of a multideck parking garage in the Bronx Terminal Market.

    “I like to take in the sights,” he said.

    Stacey White, 38, a lab technician who lives in Pennsylvania, guessed that she, her boyfriend, her younger brother and a friend had walked two miles round trip from their parking garage to get their tickets from StubHub.

    “We didn’t think it would be so far,” she said.

    Andres Gomez, 32, a hospital worker from Queens, said he would have rather used the time he spent walking to StubHub to sip a beer at a bar. Sean Connors, 29, who works in sales and lives in Kings Park on Long Island, said the same. So did Gene Sava, 28, a bartender who lives in Brooklyn. Sava added that he had gotten lost on his walk to StubHub.

    “So much drama,” he said. “I was hoping to be in a bar by now.”

    Mike Rendino, 41, the general manager at Stan’s Sports Bar, along River Avenue just south of the stadium, said he expected the StubHub situation to cost him some business. But he guessed it would cost Yankee Stadium concessionaires more.

    “I think eventually they’ll have to work it out with StubHub,” Rendino said.

    At issue is the Yankees’ decision in February to no longer accept print-at-home tickets and insist that fans not using real tickets use bar codes on their mobile phones to gain admission, something StubHub does not have access to.

    The Yankees said paper tickets had led to issues of fraud, although that claim has been met with skepticism. The team, after all, has had periodic battles with StubHub over the sale of Yankees tickets on the secondary market. To many, the ban on paper tickets seemed like an effort to impede StubHub and benefit Ticketmaster, which runs the Yankees Ticket Exchange, a club-affiliated secondary market, and has access to the mobile phone ticket codes.

    Since the Yankees’ announcement, the team and StubHub have had talks, but no agreement has been reached. Which means fans buying on StubHub need to be ready for some exercise.

    At the StubHub office on Tuesday, a greeter opened the door for Yankees fans. Nearby, Alicia Smith, 22, an event specialist, gave directions to fans wandering through the Bronx Terminal Market’s maze of sidewalks and elevators.

    “It’s freezing,” she said. “But a lot of people are willing to freeze for the Yankees.”

    For some fans, StubHub turned out to be a boon. The company ended up giving the brothers Adam Blake, 24, Max Blake, 22, and Jordan Blake, 19, of Long Island, seats in the upper deck that were seven rows better than the ones they purchased.

    Thomas Pennington, 23, a law student from Queens, bought a ticket he saw advertised at $48 on the Yankees Ticket Exchange for only $16 on StubHub.

    “I don’t mind the walk,” he said. “It’s a good deal.”

    But Marcus Quiles, 29, who lives in Staten Island and works as a traffic guard, could not understand the situation. Why could the Yankees not work out a deal with StubHub so ticket buyers could gain admission to the ballpark at the ballpark?

    “I don’t know why they make fans go to extra lengths,” he said. “It makes them seem a little greedy.”
