    Mets Win Home Opener Against the Phillies

    By TIM ROHANAPRIL 8, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Jacob deGrom was dominating the Philadelphia Phillies in the Mets’ home opener on Friday when he yanked a few fastballs in the sixth inning and felt a muscle in his back tighten up. DeGrom allowed the first run of the day, finished the inning and retreated to the dugout, debating whether he should alert someone.

    As the Mets took the lead in the bottom half of the inning and deGrom’s spot was coming up in the batting order, he told the pitching coach, Dan Warthen, about his back. It was a cold and windy day at Citi Field, and deGrom figured it would be best not to push it and injure himself further.

    “I just said something to be smart about it,” deGrom said.

    The Mets, however, did not need deGrom any longer and cruised to a 7-2 win. Even in a shortened start, deGrom struck out six batters on 76 pitches. 

    Entering the game, there were questions about whether deGrom would make this start at all. His wife, Stacey, was past due to give birth to their first child, a son. DeGrom vowed to be there for the birth, even if he had to leave for Florida in the middle of his start.

    That did not happen. DeGrom’s wife called him on Friday morning and told him that she had seen the doctor on Thursday and that it seemed unlikely she would give birth on Friday. Nevertheless, a private plane was made available for deGrom if the situation suddenly changed. And with all that in the background, deGrom went out to the mound and shut the Phillies down through the first five innings. Not one runner reached second base.

    Still, despite his dominance, deGrom’s velocity on Friday sat at about 92 miles per hour, a few ticks slower than the normal speed of his fastballs. DeGrom had struggled with the same issue during spring training, but at that point, he shrugged it off. He noted that because of his heavy workload through last year’s postseason, he had started training for 2016 later than usual, and he assured reporters that his velocity would return once the season began and his adrenaline kicked in.

    After Friday’s game Manager Terry Collins and deGrom each played down the fact that his velocity was still stuck around 92 m.p.h., repeating some of the same points from last month — that they were concerned about his results, not his velocity. 

    “That’s been a question I’ve been getting asked all spring,” deGrom said, flashing a grin. “It was cold out today. I think it’ll be back sometime soon.”  

    Said Collins, “As long as Jake feels good, he’ll get his velocity back.”

    The Phillies did not seem capable of hitting him, anyway. When Freddy Galvis swung through a deGrom fastball for strike three to end the third inning, he tossed his helmet and bat aside in disgust. When Cameron Rupp struck out looking at a fastball on the outside edge of the zone, he stood and stared at the home-plate umpire Paul Nauert for a few seconds.

    If the Mets are to repeat as National League East champions, they will probably have to play this way against division rivals. Last season, they compiled a .618 winning percentage in division games, the highest mark of any team in the N.L. East. And they particularly took advantage of the Phillies, who were one of the weaker teams in baseball last season and appear to be again this year. 

    Friday’s game began with some pomp, with three old Mets favorites — Rusty Staub, John Franco and Edgardo Alfonzo — raising the team’s 2015 National League championship flag in right field. Then Michael Conforto and Neil Walker combined to drive in five runs, Jim Henderson pitched another perfect inning, and Antonio Bastardo struck out two batters in his Mets debut.

    Perhaps the only troublesome developments were that David Wright made a throwing error — an issue that may linger as the season progresses — and that deGrom left the game early. 

    “We’ll know more tomorrow,” Collins said. “He’s pretty stiff right now.”

    When Steven Matz sustained a similar injury last season, the Mets decided to let him pitch through it. He subsequently missed about two months. The Mets would seem likely to avoid making that mistake again, which means treating deGrom cautiously, even if he indicated afterward that he was unconcerned.

    In any case, the timing of deGrom’s next start was already uncertain because of the imminent birth of his son.  

    As Collins left the news conference, he crossed paths with deGrom. “Have the baby soon,” Collins said, patting him, the final word on a pretty successful day.  

    INSIDE PITCH

    The Mets announced that Zack Wheeler will undergo a minor medical procedure on Saturday to remove a suture knot in his right forearm, an artifact of his Tommy John surgery. The Mets said Wheeler should resume throwing within a few days of the procedure and indicated that his rehab timetable would be unaffected. He is expected to return in July. ... David Wright will sit out Saturday night’s game against the Phillies at Citi Field, the first of what will probably be a steady diet of games in which he will rest in a bid to keep his back issues under control.
