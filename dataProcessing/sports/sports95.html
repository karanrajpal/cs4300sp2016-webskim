    A Hockey Bond for Father, Sons and Honorary Cousin

    By DHIREN MAHIBANAPRIL 9, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    MISSISSAUGA, Ontario — For the Nylanders, hockey is a family business, and Brooks Laich may have done enough to join it.

    Laich assisted on William Nylander’s first N.H.L. goal, for the Toronto Maple Leafs on March 5, about seven years after assisting on the final N.H.L. goal by Nylander’s father, Michael, for the Washington Capitals.

    Laich, 32, said he figured that the accomplishment should put him in the category of a “cousin or an uncle.” Michael Nylander, now 43, joked, “Maybe a brother.”

    Michael added: “It’s amazing that happened. It’s just funny how that ends up. It’s a great memory, hopefully for him, and also for me and William.”

    Michael is now trying to help his younger son, Alexander, create hockey memories of his own. Michael, a veteran of 920 N.H.L. regular-season games, retired last season after playing in Europe. He works as an assistant coach for the Mississauga Steelheads, with whom Alexander led all Ontario Hockey League rookies in scoring this season, with 28 goals and 75 points in 57 games.

    “It was a little bit funny at first, and it was a little bit different seeing your dad at the rink every day,” said Alexander, 18, who refers to his father as Michael on the bench. “It’s a lot of fun. He can help me, and he brings a lot of N.H.L. experience.

    “At the rink it’s coach and player, and at home it’s dad and son.”

    James Boyd, the head coach and general manager of the Steelheads, took a gamble by selecting Alexander at last year’s Canadian Hockey League import draft. In 2014, Boyd selected William, the Maple Leafs’ first-round pick, and was unable to persuade him to leave his Swedish club for the O.H.L.

    But with William in Toronto playing for the Leafs’ American Hockey League affiliate, the Marlies, Boyd figured he would have more success trying to get Alexander to come over.

    Alexander, who also had six goals and six assists in the playoffs, is now among the top prospects for the N.H.L. draft in June.

    His father, who played in parts of 15 N.H.L. seasons, including two with the Rangers, is enjoying life behind the bench.

    “Playing the game is totally different than coaching,” Michael said. “You have to take care of the whole team, be here, prepare practice.”

    Michael, who finished his N.H.L. career with 209 goals and 470 assists, has five of his six children living with him in Toronto.

    “It’s funny how things work out,” he said. “William is here, Alex was drafted here, and they asked me to come over here and coach. It’s such a good opportunity to have both kids in the same city.”

    He added: “They do their own thing like they always have. I do my thing, and now my family is here, whole family is here, and they fight like brothers do at home. I try to just separate them. They play video games, watch TV, relax, go to movies and hang out with their teammates. It’s normal life, and it’s an opportunity that it’s fun to see them grow up.”

    William and Alexander were born in Calgary, Alberta, where Michael spent parts of five seasons with the Flames. It was during Michael’s time in Washington that Laich got to know the family.

    Laich fondly recalled Michael bringing his children, including William and Alexander, into the Capitals’ locker room.

    “It was a team event, and Michael walked in, and he walked in with six kids,” Laich said. “Six blond-haired kids just went ‘boom’ like a grenade went off in the room, with six little bobbleheads walking around. Willy was the tallest one, so you could recognize him.”

    William, 19, said it was just one of the perks of having a father in the N.H.L.

    “We’d be all over the place; we’d come down there and do whatever,” he said. “We liked going down there. We were just kids. We didn’t really know how big it was. That’s the way we grew up, in locker rooms.”

    Maple Leafs Coach Mike Babcock said that children of N.H.L. players had an obvious advantage if they chose to get into the game.

    “I think you at least have the hockey sense because you just understand the game,” he said. “It sets you up on the fast track. Now, you’ve still got to do something with it. Talent is great if you do something with it.

    “The other thing I guess you develop is probably a passion and a love for the game. It’s a pretty good way to make a living, if you can do it.”

    William has six goals and seven assists in 21 games since being called up on Feb. 29 as part of an influx of youth for the rebuilding Leafs.

    Dennis MacInnis, the scouting director for ISS Hockey, an independent scouting and information provider on hockey prospects, said Alexander would have no trouble following William’s footsteps to the N.H.L. MacInnis has seen considerable improvements in Alexander’s game since the world junior championships in January.

    “His skill level is off the chart,” MacInnis said. “One of the issues we had, as a scouting staff, is his play without the puck had to improve, and it looks like he’s made it.”

    ISS had Alexander at No. 4 in its April rankings.

    What sets him apart from his peers is obvious, the Steelheads’ Boyd said.

    “Elite skill,” Boyd said. “Puck-handling, shooting — he’s tremendously skilled. World-class skill.”

    Alexander, who, like his brother, represents Sweden in international competition, grew up playing hockey on North American ice with the Chicago Mission and Greenwich Blues minor hockey programs, so he has not had to adjust to the smaller ice like typical European prospects.

    As Alexander prepares to take the next step in his hockey career, he knows where he needs to improve. “Get a little faster, a little quicker — things get a little quicker when you get to the next level,” he said.

    With the Leafs most likely holding a lottery pick for the June draft, the brothers have allowed themselves to consider what it might be like to play on the same team for the first time.

    “That would be insane, but we’ll have to wait and see if that happens,” William said.
