    Playing Alongside a Friend Now Known as Caitlyn

    By KAREN CROUSEMARCH 30, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    RANCHO MIRAGE, Calif. — At the 10th tee, one of Danielle Kang’s amateur playing partners greeted her with the news that she had holed out from the fairway for an eagle on Mission Hills Country Club’s par-4 first hole.

    “You would have been so proud of me,” the player, Caitlyn Jenner, told Kang.

    Kang was overjoyed for her friend but not because of any shot Jenner had hit on Wednesday during the pro-am ahead of the first women’s major of the year, the ANA Inspiration. At Tuesday night’s pro-am draw party, Kang had watched in wonder as Jenner was swarmed by well-wishers seeking autographs and snapping selfies.

    It was the first time Kang had been in public with Jenner, her longtime friend, since Jenner had become the world’s most visible transgender woman. Kang, 23, was struck by Jenner’s transformation — not in her physical appearance but in her public acceptance.

    As a teenager, Kang became close to Jenner, then known as Bruce, a member at Sherwood Country Club outside Los Angeles, where Kang honed her game. Kang gave Jenner golf tips, and Jenner, the 1976 Olympic decathlon gold medalist, offered her advice on excelling in the competitive arena. They established a routine: Kang, a two-time United States Women’s Amateur champion, would beat Jenner on the course, and Jenner would buy her lunch afterward.

    Jenner took to affectionately calling Kang, whose father died when she was young, “daughter.”

    “He was always a nice guy; he was a good friend,” Kang said.

    But her relationship with Jenner caused consternation within her circle of acquaintances. Some people did not understand why she chose to spend time with Jenner.

    “When he was Bruce, people were like, ‘Oh, that guy’s weird,’ ” Kang said, adding: “People talked bad. They always presumed all these ideas about him.”

    One afternoon when she was around 18, Kang said, she approached Jenner.

    “I didn’t like what people were saying,” she said, adding: “I didn’t know if it was true. So I confronted him. I said, ‘What’s the deal here?’ ”

    Kang said Jenner invited her to lunch at the Sherwood clubhouse and over sandwiches told her that he identified as a woman named Caitlyn and that he would on occasion dress up in a wig and women’s clothes in public at night.

    “He explained this whole deal,” said Kang, who asked Jenner if he still wished to be called Bruce. “He said, ‘Yeah,’ and I said, ‘O.K., I’ll call you Bruce until you become Caitlyn.’ ”

    Kang’s next words strengthened their friendship. “O.K.,” she said, “let’s go play golf.’ ”

    In 2013, Jenner played the pro-am of this event with Kang, who recalled, with a laugh, that he expressed disappointment for a long time afterward that their team did not win. Two years later, Jenner spoke for the first time publicly about transitioning from a man to a woman in an interview with Diane Sawyer of ABC.

    Jenner made her public debut as a woman on the cover of Vanity Fair’s July 2015 issue. Overnight, Jenner acquired scores of new friends in the transgender community. She became an instant role model for those in society who felt like outsiders.

    But she never forgot Kang’s nonjudgmental acceptance. When Jenner decided to make her pro-am debut as Caitlyn at this year’s event at Mission Hills, there was no question with whom she would play. Kang’s mother, Grace Lee, said Jenner phoned the organizers and said, “I’m not playing unless I’m playing with Danielle.”

    Jenner, who got her handicap down to a 5 before she began to transition to a woman, has seen her game change over the past year.

    “She says she putts better because of her boobs,” Kang said. “They anchor her.” She laughed. “She told me I should get a boob job.”

    Jenner’s golf coming-out party was essentially two years in the making.

    “Last year I asked her to come,” Kang said. “She was going through the whole change stuff. I said, ‘O.K., well, you can come next year, then, when you’re Caitlyn.’ ”

    Kang said Jenner almost backed out of this year’s event because of a full schedule of appointments and previous commitments, including the filming of the second season of her E! reality television show, “I Am Cait.”

    Kang said that she cajoled Jenner into rescheduling her other appointments and that she was so glad she did after seeing the positive reception Jenner received at the draw party.

    “She’s exactly the same person to me,” Kang said. “I was like, ‘Wow, people love you.’ ”

    She added, “However she has inspired them, it’s great to see.”

    One of the golfers who had her photograph taken with Jenner was Jaye Marie Green, who posted the close-up of her cheek-to-cheek pose with Jenner on her Instagram account. Green was surprised when a few of her 19,600 followers decided to unfollow her and wrote negative comments about the picture.

    Green, 22, contemplated deleting the photograph, she said, “because I don’t like reading anything negative, ever.”

    But the photograph stayed.

    “I took a picture with a human being,” Green said. “If someone’s mad about that, I can’t control what other people do in life.”

    Green added, “It kind of made me then realize what Caitlyn goes through every day.”

    Wednesday began far too early for Jenner’s comfort. She is not a morning person but was on the first tee at 7 a.m., with the pro Marina Alex, who played the first nine holes with the group of four amateurs before handing them over to Kang. The amateurs included the recently retired United States soccer star Abby Wambach, who said she took up golf less than a year ago.

    Despite telling Matt Lauer of the “Today” show in an interview last year that she intended to play from the women’s tees, Jenner did not hit from the forward tees. She shared a cart with Wambach, who kept the mood light with her high-decibel, high-energy high jinks, which included jumping into Poppie’s Pond around the 18th green while fully clothed.

    On the 12th tee, Jenner was answering questions when Wambach interjected, “She’s happy with her partner.” Jenner tried to ignore her, but Wambach kept talking. “We’re partners in crime,” Wambach said.

    Jenner joked that her partner “lacks enthusiasm.” She smiled. “We’re having fun,” she said.

    The pro-am event was good preparation for a coming women’s tournament at Sherwood in which Jenner intends to participate. “All the ladies want me to play,” she said.

    A tournament official said he had fielded more news media requests for Jenner than for the world’s No. 1 women’s player, Lydia Ko.

    “I don’t worry about popularity,” Jenner said. “I feel like I’m in a position to do more.”
