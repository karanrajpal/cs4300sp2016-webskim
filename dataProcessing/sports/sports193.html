    Yankees Lose on a Play They Couldn’t Have Prepared For

    On Baseball

    By TYLER KEPNER APRIL 5, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Baseball is a funny game, said Joe Garagiola, the old catcher and raconteur who died last month. That was the title of Garagiola’s book, and it explains so much about this fascinating and frustrating show, now playing daily for the next six months at a ballpark near you.

    Mark Teixeira, the veteran Yankees first baseman, had his own spin on the phrase after an icy opening day in the Bronx on Tuesday. “Baseball is a nuanced game,” said Teixeira, who was resigned to the Yankees’ fate: a 5-3 loss to the Houston Astros, the team that eliminated the Yankees last fall.

    Nuanced? Baseball is so nuanced that after six weeks of spring training, the Yankees lost their opener on a play they do not practice. A squibber up the first-base line, a runner on the grass, an obstructed pitcher lobbing an alley-oop far over the bag. A go-ahead run, an argument, a protest, a loss.

    The correct play, for Dellin Betances, would have been to fire the ball at the back of the runner, Carlos Correa, which would have shown that Correa had interfered with the throw. Dana DeMuth, the umpiring crew chief, said as much after the game.

    But how would a team prepare for such a play? Maybe send a clubhouse kid — or a sportswriter — up there as a sacrifice and have the pitchers drill him in the back?

    Maybe not. Maybe it was just a freak way to lose.

    “I remember years and years ago, we practiced, like, an error play, where an infielder makes an error,” Teixeira said. “And then we were like, ‘What are we doing?’ Who’s backing up where, what happens here? We’re practicing for an error. That play just doesn’t happen very much. Nine times out of 10, we get it right. It just didn’t work our way today.”

    It worked out for the Astros, who — let’s remember — won the second wild card last season, not the first. The Yankees earned their home-field advantage last October but could not score against Dallas Keuchel and the Houston bullpen. Keuchel was shakier this time, walking four in the first three innings, but he survived.

    “The baseball was so chalky,” Keuchel said. “It was like throwing a sand ball up there.”

    When Keuchel found his command, he cruised through his final four innings, sticking around long enough to earn the win. Nobody threw more innings than Keuchel in the American League last season, certainly not Masahiro Tanaka, who was lifted after five and two-thirds on Tuesday.

    The last hit that Tanaka allowed was a homer by Correa, 21, who experienced his first opening day in the majors but is already a top-10 talent in the game. The Astros have reason to be optimistic, even against Betances in the eighth, the inning he usually dominates.

    “Astro magic,” said Keuchel, with an accurate enough explanation. The Astros sent six hitters to the plate against Betances, and only one got a hit: Luis Valbuena, who could have been called out on a borderline 2-2 pitch. Instead, with a full count, Valbuena singled in two runs for the final margin of victory.

    Betances offered no excuses. His real problem, he said, was walking Jose Altuve, who scored on his throwing error.

    “You can’t walk the leadoff guy,” Betances said. “It usually hurts you, 90 percent of the time.”

    Actually, the Yankees won five of the seven games last season in which Betances issued a leadoff walk. He made a great pitch to fool Correa, but he just botched the throw to first. Finding Teixeira without hitting Correa would have required a better angle, or an uncommon touch for a pitcher-turned-fielder.

    “If you think it’s iffy, you might as well try to hit him inside the baseline,” Keuchel said. “The umpire is going to see that, pretty plain and clear. In real time, it’s hard to do. I don’t blame him for anything.”

    Keuchel is a Gold Glover, an outlier in his defensive skill. The bigger surprise would have been if Betances had processed, instinctively, that hitting Correa in the back was the right thing to do. There’s no pegging in baseball.

    Betances’ real mistake was in the wildness of his attempt. Teixeira compared it to football.

    “They don’t call pass interference if the ball’s way out of bounds,” he said.

    This principle is largely why the play was not subject to replay review. Few judgment calls are, because umpires have no way to account for how players react to situations. They have to imagine what would have unfolded had the players not responded the way they did.

    Betances’ throw had no chance of getting an out. Yes, Correa blocked his line of vision, but the uncatchable toss changed everything.

    “It happens too many times like this,” DeMuth said. “Maybe the ball was loose in his hand and it went out to right field. Maybe he intended to throw it and hit the batter in the back, but it was a bad throw. We’ve got to go by what’s there, what evidence is there, and use our judgment.”

    Astro magic, Yankee agony, one game down, 161 to go. What comes next? Probably something no one sees coming.

    “M.L.B. regular season,” Keuchel said. “A lot of stuff can happen on a daily basis.”
