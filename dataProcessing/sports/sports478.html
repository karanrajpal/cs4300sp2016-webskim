    Guatemalan Soccer Scandals Drain Fans’ Interest in Game

    By BILLY WITZMARCH 25, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    GUATEMALA CITY — Consider what representing the Guatemalan national soccer team entails at the moment. Three former top officials of the country’s soccer federation are under indictment in the United States, including one who FIFA is claiming defrauded it of several million dollars. The reigning Guatemalan champion, Antigua, has eight players facing suspension for failed drug tests; the players blame supplements that they say were provided by the club’s owner. Another league owner, also recently the interim president of the soccer federation, is the brother of an accused drug kingpin who is in jail in the United States.

    The corruption scandal that has rocked FIFA has hit soccer in the Americas especially hard, and perhaps nowhere are the problems as endemic as here. It is hard to find anyone involved in soccer who has been untouched: players, executives, owners and even journalists, 10 of whom were exposed by FIFA investigators as being on the payroll of an indicted former federation president.

    So when Guatemala played the United States here Friday night — the teams will meet again Tuesday in Columbus, Ohio — the objective of maintaining its faint hopes of qualifying for the World Cup had the feel of a first-world problem. Corruption, including three national team players who were barred for match-fixing in 2012, and mismanagement are viewed as so pervasive that even this soccer-loving country has begun to turn away from the game.

    The Friday night crowd at Estadio Mateo Flores, the 27,000-seat national stadium in the capital, was expected to be about one-third short of capacity. That could be in part a result of the game’s being played on Good Friday in a largely Catholic country, but it also mirrors the trend in Guatemala’s domestic league, where attendance has plummeted. The country’s two most popular jerseys are no longer the red of Municipal and the cream of Comunicaciones, but those of Real Madrid and Barcelona.

    “We lost our colors as Guatemalans,” said Carlos Ruiz, the former M.L.S. most valuable player, who is Guatemala’s career scoring leader. “We lost that because we lost the trust in the federation, we lost the trust in the league. That’s the problem.

    “The people don’t follow the soccer in Guatemala because they say, O.K., the federation steals money, the league sucks, the players are not professionals, the pitch on the stadiums are terrible. So you have to fix that.”

    Initial steps to do so have begun in recent months.

    FIFA recognized an interim executive committee after the arrest in January of Brayan Jiménez, the former president of the Guatemalan federation, known as Fedefut. The committee, made up of five people, is headed by Adela de Torrebiarte, a former interior minister and commissioner for police reforms who unsuccessfully ran for Guatemala’s presidency in 2011. She has also served as president of the Guatemalan national league and the club Cobán Imperial.

    As auditors have begun to examine Fedefut’s finances, several top officials — including the general secretary, his deputy and the finance manager — have been swept out, vendor contracts have been reopened for bidding, and some payments have been halted while they are being examined. Other reforms are being penciled out, with the objective of giving a voice to a broader constituency, among them male and female players, referees, regional associations and lower divisions. The committee appointed Walter Claveri as the national team coach in January, the first time in nearly a decade that the position has been held by a Guatemalan.

    “It’s important to give it some teeth,” Torrebiarte said Thursday of the reform process, in an interview at her home in a gated community. A slender 66-year-old with tightly cropped white hair, Torrebiarte chuckled at the suggestion that, in her, Guatemala had its own Margaret Thatcher.

    “Sports and politics are very complicated in Guatemala,” she said. “Still, you buy votes, promise things to people and gain money — that’s corruption. I hope we can open the congress to a lot more people so it becomes more transparent, democratic, open. I hope with these reforms and the changes FIFA makes, we can motivate people to get involved.”

    Still, she acknowledged that if it were not for outsiders stepping in — either the United States government’s investigators or FIFA — the old way of doing business was not likely to change.

    “If we don’t have support from somebody else who is stronger, we’re not going to be able to do it for now,” Torrebiarte said. “People might think, Oh, the U.S. is too involved, but at least somebody did something. It was not a secret what was going on.”

    The swift housecleaning in soccer has been the latest challenge to Guatemala’s most powerful institutions. A former president, Otto Pérez Molina, and a former vice president, Roxana Baldetti, have been in jail since their arrests last year and are awaiting trial on corruption charges that were uncovered by a United Nations-backed investigative unit. And a former army general, Efraín Ríos Montt, is awaiting retrial after his 2013 conviction for genocide during the country’s 36-year civil war was tossed out on procedural grounds.

    Juan Carlos Zapata, the chief executive of the Guatemalan Development Foundation, said that it was increasingly clear that reducing corruption would be vital for development in a country where 75 percent of people live below the poverty line.

    “There might be skepticism, but the country demonstrated last year to the world that things can be different, and that powerful politicians that most people saw as untouchable are now in prison or being investigated.”

    Soccer officials see a similar awakening in their sport.

    “People are tired, and everything that has happened has given them a voice,” said Mario Monterroso, the Guatemalan general secretary of Uncaf, the soccer confederation of seven Central American countries. “People don’t feel like sheep any more. They’re starting to ask questions: Where is the money going? Where is the money coming from?”

    Monterroso said that mismanagement had been a companion to corruption and that in many federations, the youth levels and the women’s programs had been neglected. Guatemala’s under-20 team, for example, has a regional tournament in July but does not have a coach.

    Ruiz, 36, said that he planned to retire at the end of the season to run for president of the federation in September in elections mandated by FIFA. He said he would run to give players a voice in the federation, something they have rarely, if ever, had because few ascended through the ranks once their playing days ended.

    “As players, we are not fighting just against an opponent we are playing, we are fighting against many things that are happening right now with the federation,” Ruiz said. “They don’t understand that you have to build something from the ground. Look, our government in the last year gave to the federation $1.5 million. Also, we have sponsors to pay a lot to be part of the national team. If you use that money to send coaches to different countries to prepare and bring them back to teach the kids how to play, and to have friendly games in Europe, in South America, so they aren’t scared to play against those players, you’re going to make a huge change.”

    There have been some encouraging signs since Claveri took over the team two months ago, including victories over El Salvador and Honduras. But playing against the United States, which has dominated the region along with Mexico, presents formidable problems. Then there are the others, the ones that are self-inflicted.

    “As soccer players, we enjoy playing soccer for a living, it’s what we love to do, and every time we play for the national team, it’s an honor,” said midfielder Marco Pappa, who plays for the Colorado Rapids and is the anchor of the Guatemalan team. “But it’s hard for the soccer players to continue to be positive because there’s a lot of corruption, a lot of negative energy. Sometimes all the negatives is normal, and you worry about it, it’s a big thing on your shoulders.”

    Pappa, speaking before practice Thursday, motioned toward the gates and noted that the players were not alone in this way. “The people outside,” he said. “They don’t really trust anymore.”

    Torrebiarte said she planned to address the team before Friday’s game. Though Guatemala has not beaten the United States in 21 consecutive games, she wanted the players to believe in themselves.

    “When you don’t have hope, you have nothing,” she said. “I want them to fight. I don’t play soccer, but I know the importance of soccer in this country. This feeling that we can’t do things right has to end.”
