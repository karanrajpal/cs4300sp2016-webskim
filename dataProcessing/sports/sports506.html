    Back From a Knee Injury, Federer Tries to Catch Up

    By ANDY KENTMARCH 24, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    KEY BISCAYNE, Fla. — Roger Federer will probably think twice before running a bath for his twin daughters again after what happened to his knee and to his beloved sport in his absence.

    Federer, the world’s third-ranked player, has been sidelined since undergoing arthroscopic surgery on his left knee Feb. 3 in his native Switzerland to repair a torn meniscus. During those seven weeks, the former women’s world No. 1 Maria Sharapova was suspended for doping, and the controversial issue of equal prize money for women at all tournaments came to the forefront.

    “Do you want to fill me in — how much time do we have?” Federer said when reminded Thursday at his Miami Open news conference that quite a bit had happened while he was away. “You’ve been waiting for this moment, haven’t you?”

    Federer returns to the court Friday against Argentina’s Juan Martín del Potro in the second round (he was given a bye in the first round). He expected to be asked more about his knee in his first meeting with the news media since losing to top-ranked Novak Djokovic in the Australian Open semifinals at the end of January, but after the first four questions touched on that subject, he was hit with a barrage of inquiries about the issues facing tennis.

    It did not take long for Federer’s demeanor to switch from lighthearted when he was rehashing how he twisted his knee on the way to the bathtub to a more serious tone when the topics of doping and equal prize money came up. After emphasizing that he was for equal prize money and reminding everyone that all of the Grand Slam events and tournaments like the Miami Open and Indian Wells already were set up that way, Federer turned his attention to Sharapova and concerns about doping.

    “Clearly I was very surprised,” said Federer, who has the career record for Grand Slam titles with 17. “I thought she was going to announce retirement or something, so I was completely surprised by the news. But it also shows that, you know, also the famous players can get caught in the system that seems to be working.”

    He added: “I still believe we should keep blood samples for 10 years, store them, let athletes and tennis players know that’s the case. You could be punished retroactively.”

    Federer shared his concern over the fact that he has only been tested once in 10 years in Dubai, yet in Switzerland he gets tested much more frequently. As a past president of the ATP Council for six years, from 2008 to 2014, he still is held in high regard for his leadership qualities.

    “In certain countries maybe the testing is not as serious as in Switzerland,” he said. “I’d like to see that across the board to be the same way and fair.”

    He continued: “All I can talk about is myself. I know what I take. What you take, you’ve got to be sure. That’s why I quadruple check whatever I take because I don’t want to take any chances whatsoever.”

    With this being the first time Federer has played in Miami since 2014 and only the second time in the last four years, he is eager to test his knee and continue his chase of Djokovic in the rankings. He was relieved to be asked about whether it would be better for men’s tennis to have a Djokovic-Federer rivalry or to have one dominant player and replied that he thinks both are O.K. but that he was fine with what Djokovic has been doing.

    “It’s good stories, good news, other than some other ones we heard today,” he said. “I’d rather talk about that than other things.”

    The fact that the Big Four men’s players — Djokovic, Federer, Rafael Nadal and Andy Murray — are in this tournament for only the second time since 2012 has brought even more excitement to the Tennis Center at Crandon Park.

    Djokovic, the defending champion, said he was excited about having the top men’s players in Miami and pointed to Federer as an important draw because of everything he has accomplished. “We’ll see some top-quality tennis,” Djokovic said. “I see the fans are already excited.”

    Federer has a more singular focus. “At the end, I’m going to focus on my own game tomorrow, my own mind, you know, managing my problems that I’ve had the last few months,” he said. “If I feel something tomorrow, I won’t play. It’s very simple. Expectations are really low, which is nice for a change.”

    Notes

    In women’s play, top-ranked Serena Williams was given a scare in her second-round match with her fellow American Christina McHale but held on to win, 6-3, 5-7, 6-2. McHale, who was given a wild-card entry into the tournament, trailed by 5-3 in the second set before reeling off five straight games to make the partisan Williams crowd nervous until Williams won six of the last seven to close out the match.

    Correction: March 24, 2016

    An earlier version of this article misspelled the surname of the player whom Roger Federer will face in the second round of the Miami Open. He is Juan Martín del Potro, not del Porto.
