    For Potvins, a Chance to Reflect on a Shared Islanders’ Legacy

    By ALLAN KREDAMARCH 20, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Jean Potvin made his entrance at Patsy’s Pizzeria just across Flatbush Avenue from Barclays Center, where he was greeted by his younger brother Denis, still one of the best known figures in New York sports almost three decades after retiring.

    They began playfully bantering in French, which led to laughter and hugs. Such is the constant repartee between the jovial siblings, teammates on the Islanders starting in 1973, when General Manager Bill Torrey acquired Jean from the Philadelphia Flyers and then drafted Denis several months later with the first overall pick.

    The Islanders went on to win the Stanley Cup each year from 1980 through 1983. Denis, the captain of those teams, remains part of Madison Square Garden lore courtesy of a coarse chant by Rangers fans — many too young to have seen him play — which dates to 1979, when many fans believe an injury he caused to Rangers center Ulf Nilsson cost them the Stanley Cup. 

    “I’m glad they remember Potvin’s Cups,” Denis said with a broad smile as he sipped a cappuccino. “I have four.”

    The brothers have skated and joked their way through countless hockey outposts during their playing days and subsequent careers, but this gathering last week was their first in Brooklyn in proximity to Barclays Center, the Islanders’ new home after 43 years at Nassau Coliseum. A piece of Islanders history has been in Brooklyn for a while. Jean, 66, works several subway stops from the arena as the senior vice president for giving at Catholic Charities, serving Brooklyn and Queens. A gregarious former defenseman who played parts of 11 N.H.L. seasons with five teams, Jean found the right outlet for his wit and street smarts, using them now to generate donations.

    Jean last played for the Islanders in the 1980-81 season before working as a broadcaster for the team, then leaving to pursue a 25-year career on Wall Street.

    The meeting was a rare in-season chance to commiserate with Denis, 62, who retired in 1988 and was inducted to the Hockey Hall of Fame three years later. Denis now works as a TV commentator for the Florida Panthers, who visited the Islanders last week and play the Rangers at the Garden on Monday.

    Denis admitted to having known little of Brooklyn during his Islanders days, though he had spent significant time enjoying Manhattan. Courtesy of the Panthers’ owner, Vincent Viola, a Brooklyn native, Denis said he had lately become somewhat more familiar with the city’s most populous borough.

    Talking about their time together with the Islanders provided Denis an opportunity to shower praise on his older, less famous brother, whom he affectionately calls Potsy.

    “I’m happy Jean is doing something that makes him happy and it just happens to be close to where the Islanders are playing now,” said Denis, whose No. 5 hangs from the Barclays rafters. “I’m extra proud because we had a charity golf tournament in Ottawa way back, and our dad was part of it. It’s really nice to see that spirit continuing.”

    The Potvins joined the Islanders the same season Al Arbour became coach. The brothers became emotional talking about Arbour, who died last August at 82. “Al was very hard on us both,” Denis said as Jean nodded in affirmation. “But he never dealt with us in the media and he always had our backs. And for that he always had our respect.”

    Jean recalled his first Islanders training camp, when a skin condition kept him off the ice for three days, much to the chagrin of the hot-tempered Arbour.

    “Al was screaming at me when he found out the doctor said I couldn’t perspire for three days,” Jean said with a chuckle. “He demanded I still watch every minute of practice. So I would be up in the stands, yelling that teammates like Clark Gillies weren’t giving 100 percent. The guys were firing pucks at me so I had to move higher. I had nothing to lose because I knew Al would kill me when I got back on the ice. And he almost did!

    “My teammates even honored me by starting the Jean Potvin Training Camp Award, for the guy missing time with the most idiotic, unknown or unheard-of injury or disease, those you couldn’t trace,” added Jean, who mentored young Denis as the components of the Islanders’ dynasty started coming together. “I think I won it four times in a row.”

    Jean’s best season was 1975-76, when his 72 points were third on the team behind his brother’s and Bryan Trottier’s, and second in the league among defensemen.

    In an 8-1 home victory over the Detroit Red Wings that season, Jean Potvin had a hat trick and an assist while Denis had two goals and two assists. Jean Potvin remains the only Islanders defenseman besides Denis with a hat trick. Jean was traded to the Cleveland Barons in January 1978, then played for Minnesota before returning to the Islanders as a free agent in June 1979. He played another 50 games for the Islanders over his last two seasons, winning two Cups.

    “It’s funny because throughout my years on Wall Street and now with Catholic Charities, someone will perk up when they hear my name,” Jean Potvin said. “And they’ll ask, ‘Oh you’re that Potvin?’ And I’ll say, ‘no I’m Jean, the brother,’ and they’ll say, ‘Oh, you played hockey too?’ ”

    The opportunity to share a reservoir of hockey and life stories generated an appreciative pause as Jean considered his work with Catholic Charities, which provides housing, meals and assistance for more than 100,00 people yearly in Brooklyn and Queens.

    “My hockey stories open doors and start conversations,” he said. “Working for a charity, that definitely makes a difference. This really is the perfect job for me because helping people is what life is all about.”
