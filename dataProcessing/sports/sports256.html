    Ruling on Coach’s Challenge: Inconclusive

    By TAL PINCHEVSKYAPRIL 3, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Among the rule changes that have shaken up the N.H.L. this season, nothing has been as constructive and confounding as the coach’s challenge.

    The new three-on-three overtime format drew criticism in the season’s opening weeks. But it will not be used during the postseason, and the coach’s challenge will remain as the one innovation that could decide pivotal games.

    It has already proved important in late-season games carrying playoff implications, with some high-profile players and coaches questioning the integrity of the process.

    The challenge system was instituted by the N.H.L.’s board of governors last summer to give coaches an opportunity to review scoring plays for a missed offside call or possible goaltender interference. After a coach’s challenge, a referee reviews the play on a tablet computer and decides whether to confirm or overrule the initial call.

    If a challenge fails, the team loses its lone timeout, so the decision — after consultation among coaches, players and video assistants — is not made lightly.

    “I think you want to be able to study the referees that are in the game, what their track records are,” Devils Coach John Hynes said. “For us, it doesn’t change our protocol, but it might change what type of calls you’re going to challenge or not. Every morning, we watch the challenges from the night before. We continue to see what’s getting called and what’s not getting called in the league. So we do study a little bit. Just little factors that could go into a decision in a game.”

    The N.H.L. took steps last month to improve the accuracy of reviews of offside calls, announcing at the general managers’ meetings that two cameras would be installed at each blue line to examine zone entries more closely.

    “Whatever makes it better,” Anaheim Ducks Coach Bruce Boudreau said. “It’s difficult — you see how close these things are sometimes. The linesmen do such a good job at it that the cameras can only help solidify their decisions.”

    With a regular season’s worth of empirical evidence at their disposal, N.H.L. coaches do not seem worried about offside calls. Through March 30, there were 240 challenges; 79 were reviews of offside calls, with 31 goals overturned as a result.

    A majority of challenges — 137 — concerned goalie interference, a more subjective type of ruling that has been a continuing point of contention.

    When the Los Angeles Kings star Anze Kopitar deflected the puck past Rangers goaltender Henrik Lundqvist to tie a March 17 game with 4 minutes 10 seconds left in regulation, Coach Alain Vigneault wasted little time disputing the call. Kings forward Tyler Toffoli had been standing in the crease on the play, and the Rangers expected the call to be reversed.

    But the goal was upheld by the referee Dean Morton, who ruled that Rangers defenseman Ryan McDonagh had pushed Toffoli into the crease. The Kings edged the Rangers, 4-3, in overtime on another goal by Kopitar.

    “I’m so frustrated right now, I don’t know where to begin with this,” Lundqvist said after the game. “The rule is you can’t have a guy in the crease. He doesn’t make any effort to get out, and I can’t believe, after watching it, not getting it right. That means you can pretty much put a guy in the crease, and as long as the goalie doesn’t get run over, it’s a good goal.

    “Now you get a chance to watch it. I think you should be able to get it right. That’s my opinion.”

    Before the game, Vigneault had acknowledged that difficulties interpreting what constituted goaltender interference had hindered the coach’s challenge process.

    “I think we’re all trying to understand what is goalie interference, what’s not goalie interference,” Vigneault said. “As we go through it more, I think we will have a clearer picture of what the N.H.L. is trying to do. It’s about getting it right, and there’s a learning curve. I think we’re in that learning curve, and we’re all going to get better as it moves on.”

    Of the 137 coaches’ challenges over goalie interference, only 25 resulted in goals’ being waved off.

    Early in the season, officials seemed to apply a stricter interpretation of interference, leading to more disallowed goals.

    “I think the rule has been changed from the beginning of the year, where they were calling every little thing,” Boudreau said. “Now I think it’s got to be a significant charge or bump into the goalie to call it.”

    On Feb. 20 in Edmonton, the Oilers used a coach’s challenge to contest a goalie interference call on Taylor Hall that had negated a goal. The play was upheld on review, prompting Hall to question the system, the referees’ objectivity and even the Wi-Fi at Rexall Place after the game.

    A few weeks later at the general managers’ meetings, Edmonton’s Peter Chiarelli said he supported the challenge system, though he said he did not like the potential loss of a timeout.

    “For all of us, the coach’s challenge has been a very useful tool,” Chiarelli said. “I think it’s going to prevent the egregious mistake, and there’s always going to be stuff we disagree on, but I think it’s been terrific.”

    In the final minute of regulation and in overtime, reviews are initiated by the N.H.L.’s hockey operations department, which has a video control room in Toronto. The department also reviews all goals and potential scoring plays on which it is unclear whether the puck has crossed the goal line.

    Even without the coach’s challenge, video review has affected the playoff chase in crucial ways.

    Trailing the Florida Panthers, 2-1, on March 24, the Boston Bruins thought they had tied the score 8:03 into the third period. But a review was inconclusive, as the video failed to show the puck completely crossing the goal line. Fighting for a playoff spot, Boston lost the game, 4-1, leaving Coach Claude Julien flummoxed, especially after photographs and video stills that emerged later appeared to show the puck having crossed the line.

    “You look at it and what you’re getting is feedback from your people — it’s a goal,” Julien told reporters after the game. “You’re just waiting for them to call it, and then they call it no goal. It’s baffling, to say the least. If they have the better system than we do, they can see something different. We certainly don’t.”

    With the playoffs about to begin, the stakes have never been higher for coaches to make the right call when it comes to a challenge.
