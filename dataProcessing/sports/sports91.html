    A Mentor’s Words Resonate With a New Mets Reliever

    By TIM ROHANAPRIL 9, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    When the Milwaukee Brewers demoted their struggling closer, John Axford, early in the 2013 season, they decided to let two pitchers share the role: Francisco Rodriguez, a four-time All-Star who had once saved 62 games, and Jim Henderson, who until the previous season had spent almost a decade in the minors.

    Though they were competing for innings, the sometimes hotheaded Rodriguez took the soft-spoken Henderson under his wing and made a habit of advising him after outings. Henderson flourished under Rodriguez’s tutelage, making 60 appearances and saving 28 games in 32 chances with a 2.70 earned run average.

    But Henderson’s career took a sharp detour in 2014. He developed shoulder problems early in the season and later had surgery. He missed most of the season. He spent 2015 rehabbing in the Brewers’ minor league system.

    Now, however, Henderson is back in the big leagues, having earned one of the final spots on the Mets’ roster. And he is still drawing on the advice he received from the enigmatic Rodriguez.

    “Everybody has their own judgments,” said Lee Tunnell, the Brewers’ bullpen coach, referring to Rodriguez, who was an All-Star with the Mets in 2009. “But he was really good talking with guys about pitching, because somebody did that with him. That’s his way to leave his legacy.”

    Henderson and Rodriguez played together with the Brewers during parts of the 2012, 2013 and 2014 seasons. When they first met, Henderson was making his major league debut, at 29, nine years after he was drafted in the 26th round by the Montreal Expos in 2003.

    Henderson recalled that after he pitched, Rodriguez would volunteer to discuss pitch selection and Henderson’s approach, especially if Rodriguez disagreed with what he had done.

    Henderson remembered Rodriguez telling him, “If you’re going to get beat, get beat with your best stuff.”

    Henderson said, “I would put that stuff in the back of my head, so that when I was out there the next time, I could step back and think about what he had been telling me.”

    That image of Rodriguez as a mentor stands in contrast with the image he portrayed as a Met. Fans remember him for his short temper: confronting a Yankees reliever on the field over comments reported in the news media; getting into a heated altercation with his bullpen coach; being arrested and accused of assaulting his girlfriend’s father at Citi Field after a loss.

    But Henderson described Rodriguez as a “really great” teammate, someone he could confide in and learn from. Henderson, of course, had talent to work with. His fastball touched 97 miles per hour, making his slider look that much more devastating when he counterpunched with it.

    “He really taught me how to pitch to the scoreboard,” Henderson said of Rodriguez. “Just watching him pitch, you can tell he’s really aggressive with his fastball, gets ahead of hitters when he has a cushion or a lead. Then, in one-run games, he gets nasty and bears down.”

    At the start of the 2014 season, Henderson’s velocity was down, and his shoulder was sore. He last pitched that season on May 1, had his second operation on his throwing shoulder in August, and spent all of 2015 trying to regain his edge. He signed a minor league contract with the Mets this past winter.

    Henderson said he began pushing the limits of his arm in spring training and found that his velocity had returned. He posted a 1.69 E.R.A. in 12 appearances and made the opening day roster as a late-inning reliever, smiling from ear to ear when he was told.

    The Mets could perhaps have not picked a tougher spot for Henderson’s return. Playing the reigning World Series champion Kansas City Royals on Tuesday, they brought Henderson in the seventh inning to face Alex Gordon, clinging to a two-run lead.

    Henderson struck Gordon out on a 96-mile-an-hour fastball, then retired the next two batters. He threw 13 pitches, 11 of them for strikes, a ratio that caught the eye of Manager Terry Collins. Henderson’s fastball averaged 95.5 m.p.h., about where it was before the operation.

    Henderson followed that outing with another flawless performance Friday in the Mets’ 7-2 victory against the Phillies, retiring the three batters he faced in the seventh inning, two by strikeout, while throwing only 11 pitches, nine for strikes.

    “He wanted to prove he belonged on this team,” Collins said after Tuesday’s game.

    Later, Henderson stood at his locker in a dress shirt and slacks, answering questions from reporters before heading for the team bus. He chuckled about how easy it felt — both the velocity and pitching in a tough situation.

    “Anytime you’re in a close ballgame, you have to take that closer’s experience and bring it out there,” Henderson said. “That’s what made me feel comfortable out there today. It was a very valuable experience I had in Milwaukee there, pitching in the ninth innings. It’s just a really good feeling to be out there with a lead and have the trust from the coaches.”
