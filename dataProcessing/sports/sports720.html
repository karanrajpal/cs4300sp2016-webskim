    Syracuse Was Aided by Self-Imposed Ban, but a Player Paid a Price

    By MARC TRACYMARCH 17, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    ST. LOUIS — No matter how Syracuse fares in the N.C.A.A. tournament, the only reason it is playing is that Syracuse administrators determined that last season’s team would not.

    In February 2015, Syracuse, having seen the writing of an N.C.A.A. investigation on the wall, announced a self-imposed postseason ban, effective for that season.

    That meant that in March, when the N.C.A.A. Committee on Infractions barred Syracuse from postseason play after finding multiple violations by the Syracuse men’s basketball program — including staff members doing homework for players and outsiders paying players to “volunteer” — the Orange’s absence from that season’s Atlantic Coast Conference tournament and, potentially, the N.C.A.A. tournament was treated as time served.

    The maneuver was as cautious as Coach Jim Boeheim’s signature 2-3 zone defense. The most significant penalty in the infractions committee’s arsenal — banishment from postseason play, which might have cast a pall over this season — was instead finished only two months after Syracuse announced it. That freed the Orange to make this season’s N.C.A.A. tournament, which they (barely) did.

    “One month knowing you’re not going to the tournament is one thing,” Boeheim said Thursday, defending the self-imposed ban. “To sit in Syracuse and for 10 months know we’re not going to the tournament next year, that would not have been good.”

    Boeheim, whose team faces Dayton on Friday, added: “If you’re going to take it, you got to take it. You just can’t wait.”

    The problem was that in tearing off the Band-Aid last season, Syracuse denied the senior star Rakeem Christmas, a four-year letterman, the opportunity to play in one last N.C.A.A. tournament.

    N.C.A.A. critics denounce postseason bans for harming athletes who in most cases were not even students when the violations took place. Now, self-imposed midseason bans like the one at Syracuse, and a similar one that Louisville announced last month, have come under even greater fire, mostly because of the way they exact immediate punishment on players to spare programs from paying a price a year or more down the road.

    That appears to be the path Louisville chose. Two fifth-year seniors on the Cardinals’ roster, who had transferred to Louisville for their final season, missed out on closing their careers in the N.C.A.A. tournament after the university announced a self-imposed postseason ban in February in connection with an N.C.A.A. investigation into reports that a basketball staffer had paid prostitutes to entertain players and recruits. Louisville, which was 18-4 when it announced it would skip the postseason, finished the regular season 23-8 and ranked No. 14. It would have been a shoo-in for the N.C.A.A. tournament.

    “We both feel like we don’t deserve this,” Trey Lewis, who had transferred from Cleveland State, told reporters at the time, adding, “We worked very hard and we put ourselves in a position to fulfill our dreams, and that was to play in the N.C.A.A. tournament, and we feel like that’s been taken away from both of us.”

    An N.C.A.A. working group that a few years ago established a new sanctions structure openly grappled with the effect of postseason bans on innocent players, writing in a report that its members had “struggled” with the problem. But those bans will continue. The same working group said in a 2012 report, “There is no practical way to impose meaningful penalties on an institution without affecting some individuals who may not have had any involvement in or benefited from the violations for which the institution is responsible.”

    In fact, under the regulatory regimen the group helped establish, there will most likely be even more self-imposed bans, said Gene Marsh, a lawyer at the firm Jackson Lewis who once served on the infractions committee and has advised colleges facing N.C.A.A. investigations.

    “When I was on the committee, we had a fair amount of discretion of what penalties make sense,” Marsh said. “Now, they’re far more formula driven. Given a certain set of facts, you can be more sure now than in the past that a postseason ban is going to happen.”

    That greater assuredness will theoretically lead to more self-imposed bans as universities come to understand with greater certainty that a ban may be imminent.

    Self-imposed sanctions, which are usually at the ultimate discretion of a president or chancellor, carry several advantages for universities. They display cooperation, remorse and good will, but they also rapidly put the worst penalties behind and minimize future lost ground.

    “They can go and recruit and not have that hanging over their head: ‘We already have this out of the way, that’s in the past, we’re moving forward,’ ” said Evan Daniels, who covers college basketball recruiting for the website Scout.

    For programs under investigation that do not self-impose sanctions, Daniels added, “I’m sure opposing schools will use that against them.”

    Self-imposed postseason bans can also be timed to take advantage of a middling season. Preemptively barring your team from the N.C.A.A. tournament when you may not qualify is a little like detonating a bomb where it won’t harm anyone.

    Indeed, there are prominent cases in which the failure to self-impose a postseason ban backfired. Ohio State self-imposed several sanctions but not a postseason ban in 2011, when the football team ended 6-7 (including a loss in the Gator Bowl); the subsequent bowl ban applied to the next season, when the Buckeyes went 12-0.

    More recently, Southern Methodist announced several sanctions on its men’s basketball program when it revealed that it had received a notice of allegation from the N.C.A.A. in January 2015, but not a postseason ban. Last season, the Mustangs lost in the first round of the N.C.A.A. tournament to U.C.L.A. on a questionable goaltending call. This season, they went 25-5 and finished the regular season No. 25 — and then stopped playing, since, in September, the infractions committee barred them from this year’s postseason.

    In contrast, Syracuse was probably not a tournament team last season, as Boeheim acknowledged Thursday. In fact, the Orange barely managed to slide in this season; the men’s basketball selection committee gave them the 39th seed, one of the lowest for an at-large bid.

    The Syracuse senior Michael Gbinije said Thursday that he had texted with Christmas, who now plays in the N.B.A. Development League, and that Christmas had wished his former teammates good luck.

    “After the season we had last year, this is just a gift for us,” Gbinije said.
