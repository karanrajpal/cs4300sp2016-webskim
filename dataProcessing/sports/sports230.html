    Rosberg Wins Bahrain Grand Prix for Mercedes

    By BRAD SPURGEONAPRIL 3, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    MANAMA, Bahrain — With sparks flying from the titanium underbellies of the Formula One cars in the floodlights out of the dark desert sky, with pieces of carbon fiber bodywork hurtling through the air and smoke billowing from screeching tires and a failing engine, the Bahrain Grand Prix was one of the most tightly contested and colorful races in years.

    Still, after 57 laps and an hour and a half of battling, a Mercedes car again won, for the second time this season and the eighth race in a row. But for the fifth race in a row and the second time this season, it was Nico Rosberg who won for Mercedes, not his teammate, the three-time world champion Lewis Hamilton.

    Again this year, much of the excitement at the beginning of the race was due to Hamilton making a bad start from pole position, and having to battle back from seventh position. This time, however, Hamilton was also harpooned by another car in the first corner, his car was damaged, and he was nearly knocked out of the race.

    It was the 16th victory in Rosberg’s career and his first in Bahrain.

    “Yeah, awesome guys!” Rosberg said over the team radio on the wind down lap, and he repeated the word in a post-race interview. “It has been an awesome weekend. The key was the start. I got a great breakaway and then had to control the pace.”

    Kimi Raikkonen of Ferrari finished the race in second, with Hamilton third. Raikkonen’s teammate, Sebastian Vettel, did not even start the race, as his Ferrari engine blew out on the formation lap, billowing thick clouds of smoke. It was a huge blow to the Ferrari team, which finished the series second last year, with Vettel in third place in the drivers’ championship.

    Over the weekend, Formula One debated the failure of its new Saturday qualifying system, which was designed to bring more drama to the track but has only succeeded in making qualifying less exciting. Yet the first two races this season have been more action packed and competitive than most of the races last year.

    For the first 15 laps of the race on Sunday, cars were sliding about, running into each other, losing carbon fiber bodywork, and breaking down through mechanical failures. Martin Brundle, a former driver, commenting on British television, said the race looked like a “demolition derby” at the start.

    But throughout the race it was wheel-to-wheel racing up and down the pack, with Hamilton charging up from behind to second place by Lap 37, when Raikkonen made his third pit stop. But Hamilton later dropped back to third again.

    “We had pretty good speed, but not enough to win,” said Raikkonen. “A nice weekend, but we’ll have to improve on that.”

    Hamilton’s car was hit at the first corner by Valtteri Bottas in a Williams, which damaged the front wing and the floor of Hamilton’s car, causing the British driver to lose a second per lap throughout the race, according to his team.

    One of the best results again came from the Haas cars, of the new United States-based team, which, after finishing sixth in the first race of the season, finished fifth in Bahrain, with Romain Grosjean passing car after car. Unfortunately, his teammate, Esteban Gutiérrez, who had been involved in a spectacular accident in the first race, again failed to finish on Sunday, but this time it was because his car broke down after just 11 laps.

    Other standout drives came from Max Verstappen in a Toro Rosso and Stoeffel Vandoorne, a Belgian driver in his first race, who was replacing the injured Fernando Alonso — who had cracked ribs and a lung problem from his accident in Australia — at the McLaren team. Verstappen started in 10th position and finished the race in sixth, while Vandoorne started 12th and finished in 10th, scoring a point in his first race.

    Since Hamilton took his second title in a row last October at the U.S. Grand Prix, Rosberg has driven on a different level, while Hamilton — who has been criticized for becoming distracted by his activities outside the track, playing music and attending fashion shows — has appeared to somewhat lose an edge to his driving.

    After two races, Rosberg now leads the series by 17 points with 50 points, to 33 points for Hamilton, who lies in second, while Daniel Ricciardo, a driver at the Red Bull team who finished fourth in the race, is third in the series with 24 points.

    Still, Hamilton’s raw speed remains. While the teams and organization behind Formula One continued to discuss how to make the cars faster and more exciting, Hamilton set the fastest qualifying lap ever on the track in Bahrain on Saturday. Just two years after the new, hybrid engine formula was introduced to replace the normally aspirated V8 engines of the past, the V6 hybrid cars are already faster than any of their predecessors.

    In total, five of the 22 cars failed to finish the race, with technical problems.

    “I’m just taking it race by race,” said Rosberg. “It is a good moment because we have a good car, and I’m just trying to make the most of it.”
