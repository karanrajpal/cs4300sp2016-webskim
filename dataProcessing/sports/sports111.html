    Amateur Champion Brushes Off a Bad End at Masters

    By KAREN CROUSEAPRIL 8, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    AUGUSTA, Ga. — Going into the final hole of his second round at the Masters, Bryson DeChambeau, an amateur, was poised to post the lowest round of the day. On 18, he tried a low stinger to keep the ball from getting caught up in the wind and hit over the top of it. His shot landed in a holly bush, and he had to take an unplayable lie, which led to a triple-bogey seven.

    After carding his second consecutive even-par 72, he did not rue the shots that got away.

    “It’s just a good opportunity to show my character and my grace as well,” said DeChambeau, who is in a group tied for eighth, four strokes behind the leader, Jordan Spieth. He was one stroke ahead of the world No. 1, Jason Day.

    As DeChambeau, 22, the 2015 United States Amateur champion, was answering questions after his round, Dustin Johnson, who is also at even par, came by and patted him on the shoulder and congratulated him on his play. DeChambeau also received a pat on the back from Justin Thomas, who is at plus-5, on his way to the clubhouse.

    “I’m loving it out here,” DeChambeau said. “It’s a great experience. I’m learning a lot from it.”

    Referring to his stumble at the end, he added: “I may go bang my head a couple of times on the wall. No. I’m just kidding. Thirty-six holes of golf left and anything can happen.”

    With a two-under 34 on the front, DeChambeau climbed up the leaderboard. At one point he was behind only Spieth. Asked what it was like to look up at the scoreboard and see his name near the top, DeChambeau said, “I know I belong.”

    WATSON SAYS FAREWELL There will be no more majors for Tom Watson. After playing his last British Open in 2015 at St. Andrews, he played his last round at the Masters on Friday, missing the cut at age 66 at the place where he won in 1977 and 1981.

    “Glad I don’t have to hit the 5-woods and 3-woods at 18 anymore,” Watson said. “That’s the reason I’m not playing here anymore. These kids are hitting it up there, and they are hitting 7- and 8-irons on 18. And I’m back there trying to hit a 3-wood on that green.”

    Watson, who shot a 74 on Thursday and a 78 on Friday, has been doffing his cap and acknowledging the crowd’s applause for years, and his days of teeing it up at Augusta National are probably not done, with him likely to step into the role of honorary starter.

    “I had to learn how to win,” said Watson, who played the Masters 43 times. “Took me a while. Then I got on a golf course like this one, which fit my golf game well. Could have run the tables a few more times here. I was very close a number of times in this tournament, but grateful I won it twice and to be able to play it for so many years.

    “You know what else I’m grateful for is the fact that they allow the past champions to pick the time they say, ‘No mas,’ to retire,” he said. “I know a few years back, there was some talk about maybe setting an age for retirement. But it didn’t work. I think we know when it’s time to say, ‘No mas.’ ”

    CHRISTOPHER CLAREY
