    Is Formula One Still on Top?

    By BRAD SPURGEONMARCH 18, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Suggesting that Formula One has lost some of its luster with its new, quieter engines and the domination of the series by Mercedes is something of a fashion these days. But as the series begins its 67th season, at the Rolex Australian Grand Prix in Melbourne this weekend, there are indications that Formula One remains not only the pinnacle of world motor sports, but may well be at its highest point since its inaugural race, in 1950 in Silverstone, England.

    This year, for the first time, the series will hold 21 races, up from 19 last year, and will enter new territory, with the European Grand Prix in Baku, Azerbaijan, in June. The new team Haas F1, the first American Formula One team in 30 years, is joining the series, and Renault, the French automobile group, is returning a team to the series, aiming to challenge Mercedes.

    It is true that Bernie Ecclestone, the chief executive of the series, recently expressed frustration to the media about the direction that the sport — which he has built up and loves — is taking. He was quoted as saying that he would not buy tickets for his family to attend a race.

    But the chief executive of Renault, Carlos Ghosn, who is known to keep close tabs on his budgets, praised the sport as a cost-effective global marketing tool.

    “Our marketing specialists looked at how much you have to pay to make the awareness of the brand grow in certain countries,” Ghosn said at the revived Renault team’s introduction in February, alluding to countries where the company is hoping to break in, like China, India and Russia.

    “And we know that Formula One is one of the ways to make the brand progress in these countries,” he added, “and we compared the cost of racing in Formula One to the cost of marketing to improve the visibility in those countries. We know that Formula One pays for itself in those countries — as long as people see in you not just as a participant, but someone that can win in the races.”

    Despite his recent comments, Ecclestone nonetheless praised the series in a foreword to his annual Global Media Report. “One thing is for sure,” he wrote. “The coming season will unquestionably feature the highest class of motor racing on the planet and will offer the drama and excitement that only Formula One can deliver.”

    Television audience figures have fallen over all in the last two years, but Formula One is still one of the most widely watched and popular sporting series. According to Ecclestone’s report, which was prepared by independent sports marketing research agencies, Formula One attracts more than 400 million viewers around the world. It is shown in more than 150 countries through 115 broadcasting partners, totaling 41,945 hours of coverage.

    Amid the recent criticism of the series, the German Grand Prix was dropped from the calendar last year. (It is returning this year, however.) The 2015 race was not held in part because of financial problems involving the local promoter, but also because the race in 2014 attracted some of the fewest spectators of any Grand Prix in the season.

    But the Global Media Report found nonetheless that broadcasting Formula One races in the German market was a boon for RTL, one of the top three television channels in the country. Its audience share increased by 160 percent and the audience of the nearest competitor fell to around half of RTL’s, the report said.

    In general, the report found, “broadcasters without F1 rights, that traditionally dominate non-F1 Sundays, then see their audience share decimated and viewers flock to the channel or channels showing coverage of a live race.”

    The drop in television viewers in some countries has also coincided with the decision to broadcast more via pay television, away from the traditional free-to-air coverage. In some countries, however, there have been considerable increases in viewing numbers. In Mexico, for example, where the series returned last year, the figures rose more than 500 percent for certain races from 2014 to 2015.

    Whereas for decades the series boasted that its cars were the fastest moving billboards in the world, today the business dynamic has changed, according to a report published this week by Repucom, a leader in sports marketing research.

    “Team, driver and race sponsors currently invest just under £1 billion every year into the sport, a figure which has remained largely unchanged for the last three seasons,” the report said. “However, the type of companies investing in F1 has shifted from those targeting the consumer to companies selling to other businesses, with industries such as IT hardware and industrial goods providers becoming more prevalent. At the same time, whereas once brands’ engaging in F1 were largely based around sponsorship and branding, they are now increasingly concerned with creating partnerships.”

    The Repucom report also suggests that the pay-television model can increase exposure of a sponsors’ brand. Through the extended coverage offered to pay-TV viewers, including increased content before and after races, the leading teams have seen a 50 percent increase in the exposure of their sponsors. And the teams in the rest of the pack — those that never finish on the podium — can “exceed the sponsor exposure value they receive through the on-track coverage,” Repucom found.

    Some have criticized the series for what is perceived as a lack of savvy in its use of social media to attract younger fans. But using the example of the traditional Formula One country of Italy to analyze fan demographics, the report shows that interest drops off after the age of 45 and is stronger in the 4-year-old to 14-year-old age group than in the 25-to-34-year-old age group.

    Ultimately, Formula One remains the richest, most dazzling series with the most advanced car racing technology, performing on the best tracks with the best drivers.

    “Formula One is a sport — but it is also entertainment,” said Toto Wolff, the head of Mercedes-Benz Motorsport. “Controversy, both on and off track, makes headlines. That’s not a bad thing — it’s part of the world we live in.”
