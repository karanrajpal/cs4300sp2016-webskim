    A Spring of Discontent for Canadian Hockey

    By IAN AUSTENAPRIL 2, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    OTTAWA — Canada, where hockey passion is so intense that weddings on rinks are a thing, will not have a single team in the N.H.L.’s Stanley Cup playoffs. It’s as if Argentina were shut out of the world’s premier tango competition, or France had no representation in the Michelin Guide.

    To call it a national crisis would be unfair. It’s worse than that — at least according to many hockey die-hards here whose lives revolve around the playoffs each spring. What has happened to us, they wonder. And what do we do now?

    “I told my wife recently that I’ll have a lot of free time this spring,” said Geoff Cantello, a lawyer and hockey fan in Ottawa.

    Sixteen of the league’s 30 teams make the playoffs, which begin April 13. By last week, all seven of the teams based in Canada had been eliminated from contention. Entering the weekend, of the six N.H.L. teams with 70 or fewer points, five were Canadian.

    Playoffs without a Canadian team had not happened in 46 years, when there were only two Canadian teams in a 12-team league, and when the current N.H.L. players, and possibly some of their parents, had not been born.

    But the plight of Canada’s professional hockey teams is not entirely new. No Canadian team has won the Stanley Cup since 1993.

    “No Canadian teams, that’s huge,” said Adam Godard, a long-suffering fan of the Toronto Maple Leafs. “Things definitely have got to be blown up. Things have got to change.”

    Hockey is not dying in Canada. (See Olympic gold, 2014, 2010.) But a confluence of factors is giving American teams an edge in the professional league (and “American” is a loose term here, given that Canadian players populate every roster).

    Sixteen of the 21 finals since the Montreal Canadiens became the last Canadian team to win the Stanley Cup have been all-American affairs. This allows for some gloating, a rarity for Americans on the rink. (See Olympics, 2014, 2010.)

    Last month, even President Obama joined in, ribbing the prime minister of Canada, Justin Trudeau, whose father held the job the last time Canada was shut out of the playoffs.

    When Obama was asked at a news conference with Trudeau which country played better hockey, he said: “Where’s the Stanley Cup right now? I’m sorry, is it in my hometown?” (Yes, Mr. President, it is, with the Chicago Blackhawks.)

    Canadians offer many explanations for the drought, from serious to conspiratorial. The current favorite theory is that the now-low value of the Canadian dollar makes it difficult for Canadian teams to attract top talent paid in American currency.

    But whatever the cause, the league and its Canadian broadcast rights holder, Rogers Communications, are waiting to see if an absence of patriotic favorites will prompt Canadians to find new rituals this spring.

    “I think a lot of people will be like the hockey players: getting their golf clubs out and hoping for the weather to improve,” Cantello said.

    As if the drought were not bad enough, consider that some ice-challenged places south of the border are doing better: Three teams from California, two from Florida and teams from Texas and Tennessee will very likely make the playoffs.

    On Tuesday night, four days after the Vancouver Canucks were mathematically eliminated from the postseason, disengaged fans watched the Canucks fall to the playoff-bound San Jose Sharks, 4-1, dropping Vancouver at the time to a three-way tie for the league’s worst record. Five seasons ago, the Canucks enjoyed a magical run to the Stanley Cup finals, making them the last Canadian team to qualify.

    Last week, in what was once considered among the loudest arenas in the N.H.L., there were clusters of empty seats.

    “This season has been a little boring, to be honest with you,” said Jessica Bedi, 29. “We’re season-ticket holders and we go to the games only because we have to. We try to sell tickets sometimes, but people just don’t want them.”

    Of course, not all Canadian hockey fans support Canadian hockey teams, even in good times.

    Sometimes their reasons are geographical. Windsor, Ontario, tends to root for the Detroit Red Wings, just across a river, rather than the Maple Leafs, a four-hour drive away in Toronto.

    Some fans pick teams based on favorite players. Jack Jedwab, the president of the Association for Canadian Studies, said he believed that was increasingly true among younger fans, like his 19-year-old son, who lives in Montreal but is a Tampa Bay Lightning supporter because he likes a star player, Steven Stamkos, who is from suburban Toronto.

    To Jedwab, the eclipse of Canadian teams is an inevitable consequence of the sport’s growth through American expansion.

    “We’re a bit of a victim of our success,” Jedwab said.

    The result has been particularly traumatic in Montreal.

    “We’re adjusting easily to the Montreal Canadiens not being the legends of old,” Jedwab said. “We think of them as a team in a separate universe. Things have changed; those days are long gone. I hate to say this as a Montrealer: They’ve become like all the other teams.”

    Jedwab said he was struggling over the question of whether to watch the playoffs. Professional basketball seems more interesting lately, and he finds himself “really caught up in the Raptors thing.”

    “It’s a more compelling story,” he said.

    Godard, who lives about 90 minutes from Toronto when traffic is light, also finds his attention drawn to the N.B.A.’s Raptors, and was in their arena, Air Canada Centre, on Wednesday when they defeated the Atlanta Hawks, 119-100. The Raptors lead the N.B.A.’s Atlantic Division, and they have clinched a berth in the N.B.A. playoffs.

    Inevitably, the Canadian N.H.L. teams’ woes have fed a broader debate about hockey’s future in Canada. Pessimists add the teams’ shutout to a list that also includes growing concerns among parents about concussions in hockey as well as children’s increasing participation in soccer and basketball.

    Still, despite his newfound enthusiasm for the Raptors, Jedwab is not among those forecasting a decline in hockey’s prominence in Canada.

    After all, last year, five Canadian teams were in the playoffs (and the year before that, only one, but we won’t mention that.)

    A nation that powers through harsh winters won’t be checked so easily by a particularly bad sports one.

    “It’s not going to effect a big identity deficit for Canada,” Jedwab said.

    Gerald Narciso contributed reporting from Vancouver, British Columbia.
