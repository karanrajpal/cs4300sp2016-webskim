    It’s Official: Smokeless Tobacco Is Out

    By SETH BERKMANAPRIL 6, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Yankee Stadium got a new addition — or, more accurately, a subtraction — on Wednesday. Smokeless tobacco, long part of baseball’s backdrop, can no longer be used by players, coaches and managers while they are at the Yankees’ ballpark or at Citi Field, the home of the Mets.

    Mayor Bill de Blasio signed legislation prohibiting the use of the tobacco at New York City sports and recreational facilities that issue tickets. Amid the influx of new rules in baseball, not only can a Yankee or a Met no longer take out the shortstop by sliding wide of second base, he could cause his team to be subject to a city penalty if he were to use smokeless tobacco before, during or after the game.

    The ban went into effect immediately, making Wednesday night’s game between the Yankees and the Houston Astros the first time players were prohibited from using the substance at a major league game. Eventually, Yankee Stadium and Citi Field will include signage throughout the parks publicizing the ban.

    During a bill-signing event at City Hall, de Blasio and supporters repeatedly emphasized the influence wielded by professional athletes and said that removing smokeless tobacco from use might keep younger fans from picking up the habit.

    Right before de Blasio signed the bill into law, Yankees and Astros players were mixed in their reaction to the news. No clubhouse signs had yet been posted alerting team members to the ban; on the other hand, there were no visible containers of smokeless tobacco in any player lockers, either.

    Astros infielder Matt Duffy said he had learned of the forthcoming ban during spring training but thought it would affect only a handful of players. “I don’t use it,” he said.

    His teammate Preston Tucker, an outfielder, said he had not known about the new measure until a reporter informed him of it.

    “As far as the rules and regulations for players, I’m not sure, but I think it’s a good thing,” Tucker said. “I think healthwise it’s a huge step forward.”

    Yankees Manager Joe Girardi said players who had used smokeless tobacco would simply have to adapt to the new environment.

    “Things in life change all the time,” Girardi said. “And as people, we have to make adjustments. Have I heard players complain? Not really. I really haven’t. I haven’t had a player come to me and say: ‘What am I going to do? I need this.’ ”

    One area of confusion about the law has been its enforcement. Would city workers dress undercover in Brian McCann jerseys to see if any players were breaking the rule in the dugout?

    Not likely.

    “It will be interesting to see just like how they police it and how they do that type of stuff,” said Yankees outfielder Dustin Ackley, who added that he had become sick after trying smokeless tobacco in high school and had not used it since. “A lot of guys do sunflower seeds in the game, and it might look like they’ve got something in their mouth. It’s tough. It’s going to be a tough thing to kind of get control of, I feel like.”

    The New York City Health Department is supposed to respond to complaints and conduct inspections, and teams are liable if someone on the club is found to be ignoring the ban. The ban also includes arenas such as Barclays Center and Madison Square Garden, although basketball players, in particular, are far less associated with the use of smokeless tobacco than their baseball counterparts are.

    “The Mets and Yankees we expect will be fully compliant with this law,” Dr. Mary T. Bassett, the city’s health commissioner, said during Wednesday’s bill signing.

    City Councilman Corey Johnson, who, like the mayor, is a Boston Red Sox fan, said the law would “stop the flow of free advertising” of smokeless tobacco in ballparks. As he spoke, the Yankees took batting practice, launching home runs toward a large sign advertising beer in right field.
