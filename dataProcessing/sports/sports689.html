    Against a Stacked Deck, Some Midmajors Do March On

    Sports of The Times

    By WILLIAM C. RHODEN MARCH 18, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    DENVER — Josh Hagins celebrated his 22nd birthday on Thursday by giving every college basketball fan in America a gift: a tremendous upset on the first day of the N.C.A.A. tournament.

    Hagins scored 31 points, including a tying shot at the end of regulation, as 12th-seeded Arkansas-Little Rock knocked off Purdue, 85-83, in two overtimes.

    Upsets and underdogs come in all shapes and sizes. About a half-hour before Little Rock’s win, Yale, the Ivy League champion, which had waited 54 years to return to the tournament, won its first game in the event, over Baylor. Hours later, Gonzaga whipped another higher seed, Seton Hall, though it was apparent by intermission that the seedings in that matchup were simply numbers.

    “For the fans, for the media, No. 6 versus No. 11 is an upset,” Gonzaga guard Eric McClellan said. “For us, it was a game we should have won.”

    The true upset on the opening day of the tournament was the one pulled by Little Rock, a team that was completely turned over by a first-year coach who brought in 10 players this season.

    Purdue, a muscle-bound team from the Big Ten, was supposed to be better than average. The Boilermakers were taller and bigger inside than Little Rock. But it appeared early on that the Purdue players were competing against the unfamiliar name on the Little Rock jerseys, not against the players inside them, especially after Purdue had built a 12-point lead.

    Hagins shot 1 for 5 in the first half, for 2 points. Then he caught fire, ending a hot second half with a step-back 3- pointer in regulation that sent the game to overtime. The fight continued into a second overtime. Little Rock emerged and will face Iowa State on Saturday.

    “I wasn’t going to sit there and just let us lose,” Hagins said. “I wasn’t going out like that. A lot of midmajor teams don’t get this chance. I waited 22 years just to get to this one game.”

    I spent the last two weeks at midmajor conference tournaments in Las Vegas: the West Coast Conference’s, which Gonzaga won, and the Mountain West’s, which Fresno State won.

    The persistent question from one season to the next is whether the wall between the midmajor conferences and the Power 5 conferences will ever be knocked down, or even consistently cracked.

    Despite wins by Gonzaga, Little Rock and Yale on Thursday in the N.C.A.A. tournament’s round of 64, the distance between them that’s got and them that gets in men’s basketball is widening each season. And them that’s got want to keep it that way. And why not?

    “They’re getting the cream of the crop in the recruits,” said Tim Cluess, the coach of Iona, a Metro Atlantic Athletic Conference team that lost to Iowa State in the first round. “We may have a guy here and a guy there who can match up with that, but from 1 to 13, they’re a lot better and deeper.”

    He added, “They’re physically 30, 40 pounds heavier, and they’re faster.”

    Matt Painter, the Purdue coach, agreed: “You’re a little bigger, you get a little better player.”

    And around tournament time, a midmajor like Monmouth would say, the selection committee can compound the problem by not rewarding teams that excel despite these built-in disadvantages, either by underseeding them or by passing them over completely.

    “I think what the committee thinks is, O.K., if you were going to play one game, which team would you pick to win that one game if you had everything depending on it?” Cluess said. “Unfortunately, when you do that, most of the time they’re going to end up with the bigger-name school.”

    That’s because the bigger programs have better players, more resources to pay coaches, more support personnel to keep the players eligible and awesome buildings in which to have them train. But there is no way to account for a player like Hagins, a diamond in the rough who did not come out of high school ready-made.

    “When they get to be 22 years old, there’s your difference,” Painter said, referring to Hagins. “That kid is 22 years old. He’s in his fourth year, and he’s as good as anybody. Where he was at 18 is the difference.”

    Hagins had been on the verge of committing to Creighton, he said, when the coaching staff offered the scholarship to another player. Hagins found Little Rock; Little Rock found Hagins. “It’s worked out,” Hagins said.

    The difference between a midmajor and high-major player is the journey he takes to get there. The blue-chip recruits are ready-made, polished and ready to compete, often by age 18.

    “The difference might be that they get all the big recruits, and some of the small schools got to get some players who have had to take different journeys to get there,” Little Rock forward Roger Woods said after Thursday’s big win.

    Woods, who scored 13 points in 41 minutes on Thursday, attended Provine High School in Jackson, Miss. He had a new coach in his senior year, then went to Southeastern Louisiana before transferring to Mississippi Gulf Coast Community College. Finally, he was recruited by Little Rock.

    Continue reading the main story

    “I’ve had six different coaches in the last six years,” Woods said. The journey has made him a better, more mature player.

    “It’s a mixture,” he said. “Maturity finding the right game, finding a coach who trusts you, finding the right system that fits your style.”

    As Iona’s Cluess left the arena Thursday, I asked him what he thought it would take for a midmajor team to break through the granite wall in the N.C.A.A. tournament — this year or ever.

    “You have to be a senior-laden team as a midmajor or low major to have a chance, and you got to catch lightning in a bottle that day,” he said.

    The Little Rock Trojans were king for a day. “Everybody wants to be this team,” Hagins said. “Everybody wants to be the Cinderella team in the dance. For us to actually do what we’re doing right now means everything. But we’re not finished.”

    Regardless of what happens Saturday against Iowa State, on Thursday Little Rock became the horse we all want to ride.
