    As the Sabbath Nears in Israel, Soccer Becomes a Test of Faith

    By SAM BORDENMARCH 24, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    JERUSALEM — The halftime whistle blew, and Shimon Levy gathered his belongings. While most fans at Teddy Kollek Stadium converged on the concession stand for a soda or a coffee, Levy walked past the line and toward the exit. It did not matter that this second-division soccer game between Hapoel Jerusalem and Maccabi Herzliya on a Friday afternoon was delicately poised — tied at 0-0 after a handful of chances for both sides — and it did not matter that Levy had come to watch his son, who plays for the home team. Levy said he had no choice but to leave.

    “Shabbat begins in less than an hour,” he said, shrugging. “My son understands.”

    Levy’s abrupt departure was not unique. A series of circumstances involving television rights, scheduling logistics and stadium availability has led many religious soccer fans in Israel to leave matches early this season, if they go at all, in an effort to ensure that they are able to properly observe the Jewish Sabbath, which begins at sundown on Fridays.

    Daniel Cohen, another Hapoel Jerusalem fan, who followed Levy out the gate minutes later, expressed a common sentiment when he asked angrily, “Why can’t they just start an hour earlier?”

    One reason is simple enough to understand: money. The Israeli Professional Football League governing body, which oversees the country’s top two soccer divisions, has an agreement with a television outlet to broadcast league matches in certain windows, one of which starts on Friday at 3 p.m. The fallout from that decision, however, is more complex: Beginning games at that time, particularly in winter, means running perilously close to sunset. For religious fans who do not drive or use electricity, including cellphones, on Shabbat, getting home to be with their families on time often requires a halftime exit.

    The plight of those fans is the latest flash point in a long-running debate in Israel regarding sports on the Sabbath generally. For decades, the soccer federation had an unofficial, look-the-other-way exemption to stage games on the Sabbath, when many Israeli businesses are prohibited from operating and public transportation systems like buses do not operate. But last fall, a group of about 200 players protested the practice, claiming it was unfair for the league to force them to choose between the sanctity of the Sabbath and their obligations to their teams.

    The dispute took several unusual turns, most notably when a judge ruled that the government needed to officially validate the exemption and the minister in charge of such exemptions declined to do so because he is a member of an ultra-Orthodox political party. But despite the attorney general’s issuing a temporary exemption to help avert a league shutdown, the debate remains contentious both in the courts and in the country’s expansive sports community.

    The issue has several layers. At the highest level, it affects not only religious fans but also players like Guy Dayan, a midfielder for the first-division club Hapoel Acre. Dayan is one of many players who frequently miss their teams’ final pregame meetings before Saturday games. When Hapoel Acre plays on a Saturday, he stays at a different hotel — one that is within walking distance of the stadium. After a match, Dayan, who said he became more religious about six years ago, walks back to his hotel to wait for the Sabbath to end while his teammates leave the stadium on a bus.

    Dayan defended his choice to play at all on Shabbat — something many Orthodox Jews would not do because work is prohibited — by saying he was simply doing what he needed to do to provide for his family. “This is my job,” he said in Hebrew during an interview at his home in Beit She’an. “Twenty years ago, religious people would quit or only play in the third division, which didn’t have games on Shabbat. But things are different now.”

    Demographically, at least, that is the case, as the ranks of the religious have grown across Israel. Dov Lipman, a former member of the Israeli parliament who has worked to bridge the cultural gaps between observant and secular Jews, said he believed that this group was pushing for a re-evaluation of the established notion that competitive sports were primarily the domain of secular Israelis.

    In the past, religious Jews often discouraged their children from participating in organized sports because of the inevitable conflicts with the Sabbath. “But that group is now driving the issue because they want to be involved,” Lipman said. “Their ability to excel in sports is very much stunted because of Shabbat, and they are not content with that.”

    Lipman added that the issue was one of many related to the overarching question of what it means to live in a Jewish state. “Israel is only 68 years old, and we have been fighting for our existence from the beginning,” he said. “The things that go with living — sports, the Sabbath, how it all fits together — is still in the baby stages.”

    Clouding things further is the reality that many proposed solutions would involve scheduling more games on Sunday, a workday and school day in Israel. Mr. Lipman said he could see a situation in the future in which the government considers altering the current Sunday-to-Friday-afternoon workweek, but he conceded that adding a second rest day to Israeli society would be far from easy.

    For now, the argument continues. Dayan said the players had wide support, and he claimed that there were even players on the country’s biggest teams, like Maccabi Tel Aviv, Maccabi Haifa and Be’er Sheva, who supported the protests against Shabbat-intruding matches but were afraid to speak out for fear of retribution from their clubs. If the matter is not resolved this spring, Dayan said, the players plan to protest again. They may even consider a strike, he said, at the start of next season.

    Kobby Barda, a spokesman for the Israel Premier League, said the league would comply with whatever the government decided, but he questioned the depth of the players’ religious convictions (“Why do they play on Shabbat at all then?”) and said the complaints were wholly unreasonable. Beyond the fact that playing all games outside the Sabbath would require the construction of new stadiums — at least three clubs currently share Teddy Kollek Stadium here — the precedent of playing soccer on Saturday “existed here before Israel even existed at all; most people just want to keep things as they have been for the past 100 years,” Barda said.

    He continued: “If you have taken the burden of being a religious person, there are a lot of things you are losing. You have to give up so many things, and that is your choice. If you are a professional player, you need to be wherever you are told to be at the right time. If the game is at 12 p.m., you play at 12 p.m. That is what being a professional player is. We don’t have any room in the current situation to negotiate.”

    That leaves the players and the league at odds, and the fans — some of them, at least — unsatisfied. Levy’s early departure meant he did not see the two red cards issued in the second half of the heated game between Hapoel Jerusalem and Herzliya or the beautiful goal that gave Hapoel a surprising 1-0 victory.

    Gary Ginsberg could relate. Earlier this season, he left at halftime of a game between Hapoel Rishon LeZion and his favorite club, Hapoel Katamon. After rushing home, showering and dressing for Shabbat dinner, Ginsberg sneaked one final glance at his phone before the Sabbath began.

    “We were 1-0 down when I left, and we scored three times in the second half and won, 3-2,” he recalled with a shake of his head. “It was a good game, I guess.”

    Raphael Gellar contributed reporting.
