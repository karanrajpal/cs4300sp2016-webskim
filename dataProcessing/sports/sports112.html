    Ernie Els Awakens From His First-Round Nightmare at the Masters

    On Golf

    By KAREN CROUSE APRIL 8, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    AUGUSTA, Ga. — On the practice green before his round on Friday at the Masters, Ernie Els made 25 of 28 putts from inside 9 feet. The last one he struck before making his way to the first tee at Augusta National Golf Club was a 2-footer that dropped in the hole.

    Several minutes later, Els stood on the first green, over another 2-foot putt. On the same hole in the first round, Els took six putts from 3 feet for an opening nine on his scorecard, sinking his title hopes when his 22nd Masters had barely shoved off the dock.

    This putt, his second, was for a bogey. It was a tap-in, but Els was torn between marking the ball and giving himself time to regroup or trying to minimize the pain by acting quickly, as if he were removing a bandage from a wound. Opting for expediency, Els hit the putt. It missed the hole completely. He made the comebacker for a double-bogey six.

    On Thursday, Els took 39 putts, 14 more than the first-round leader, Jordan Spieth, and posted an eight-over-par 80 to sit 14 strokes back. He rebounded with four birdies in his round of one-over 73 in a howling wind Friday to finish at plus-nine, three strokes above the cut line. Els, a former world No. 1, matched the second-round score of the current world No. 1, Jason Day, who was in his group.

    “He came back and played great golf,” Day said of Els. He added, “It shows a lot of heart how much he has to be able to come back out and still fight.”

    After the first round, Els talked about “snakes and stuff going up in your brain.” At 46, he is at the age when one’s putting becomes susceptible to involuntary hiccups, more commonly known as the yips. Last season, Els failed to record a top-10 finish on the PGA Tour for the first time since 1992, when he was 22, an aberration he attributed to poor putting.

    In desperation, Els switched to a left-hand-low putting grip, and saw signs of progress. In one of his first starts of the season, in South Africa, he said he had a 100 percent success rate from inside six feet. Before a tournament in Dubai in February, Els, a four-time major winner from South Africa, said of his absent touch on the greens, “I feel that can’t really leave you. If you’re good, it doesn’t really leave you. You’ve got to prove it to yourself.”

    Earlier in the week, Els said, he decided to experiment again with his putting stroke. He made his posture more erect, and swung his putter like a pendulum. The putter felt uncomfortable in his hands, Els said, and he was hitting up on the ball instead of hitting down on it as he did before. Still, he put the stroke into play Thursday.

    “Working on something new backfired on me,” Els said. The anxiety he felt over putts in the first round, he said, might have been a function of his brain “telling me this is not normal, kind of goes against it, just went haywire.”  

    He added, “You play long enough, you make a fool of yourself somewhere, but I did it on the biggest stage.”

    By returning Friday to stare down the 445-yard first hole with his wounds from Thursday’s beating still raw, Els earned the respect of the patrons in the bleachers at the practice facility. They warmed the cool morning air considerably with their applause when he showed up on the range before his round.

    “He really deserved that,” said the 51-year-old Davis Love III, who was hitting balls when Els appeared. “Because he could have just turned right off of one instead of left and said, ‘Enough’ yesterday and nobody would have thought any worse of him for it.”

    Els said he appreciated Love’s words. But it was as if his own embarrassment eclipsed the sunbeams from the patrons and his peers. He described the scene at the range as “a very weird, surreal feeling” and said, “It was like I walked onto the range and even the players and caddies, they kind of just looked at me as if I don’t have pants on or something.”

    Olin Browne, a three-time winner on the PGA Tour who plays on the Champions Tour, said there wasn’t a player on that range — or any range, for that matter — who hasn’t experienced “something akin to what happened to Ernie.”

    Browne knows better than most exactly how it felt to be Els. He carded an eight on the first hole during the second round of his first Masters, in 1998. He missed the cut, saving him from having to face the bully of a hole the next day.

    “When that kind of thing happens it’s just gruesome,” Browne said Friday by telephone. He added, “You start to feel like a kicker who has missed three field goals in a row.”

    Els might have been guilty of projecting his own feelings onto his fellow players when he said, “They’ve got a reason to look at me funny for what happened yesterday.”

    Els’s wife, Liezl, walked both rounds with her husband. She wasn’t looking at him funny. “To me, as his wife, what happened after he walked off No. 1 in the first round made my admiration for him grow by leaps and bounds,” she said, adding, “What he showed our children when he kept playing makes me so proud.”
