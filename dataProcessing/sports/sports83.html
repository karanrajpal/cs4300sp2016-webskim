    Jordan Spieth’s Lead Disappears

    By KAREN CROUSEAPRIL 9, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    AUGUSTA, Ga. — The wind played through the Masters on Saturday, blowing sand out of the bunkers and debris onto the greens. It blew the ball of one competitor, Billy Horschel, off the 15th green and into a water hazard, costing him a penalty stroke. The wind was so fierce, it blew Jordan Spieth’s Masters crown askew.

    Spieth, the reigning champion, battled strong gusts, sleek greens and his swing to piece together a two-under par score to steer four clear of the field through 16 holes of his third round at Augusta National. On the final two holes, though, his round upturned like an old umbrella in a stiff breeze. He hit wayward tee shots at Nos. 17 and 18, and compounded his misery with poor wedges, which he usually wields like talismans to ward off bogeys. He played the final two holes at three over. His lead all but disappeared.

    Spieth finished with a one-over 73, for a 54-hole total of three-under 213, to remain one stroke ahead of Smylie Kaufman, who birdied three of his final six holes on his way to a 69, the low round of the day.

    “If I’m at five or six under, even just saying that right now, I can’t think that way,” Spieth said. “That certainly brings anyone who is over par almost out of the tournament. And now, with very little wind tomorrow, someone gets on a run and shoots six or seven under, you know, I know I have to shoot a significant under-par round tomorrow in order to win this tournament, when I could have played a different style of golf like I did on Sunday last year.”

    Spieth’s expected Duel in the Dogwoods with Rory McIlroy, with whom he was grouped in the final pairing, never materialized. McIlroy did not make a birdie and had a double bogey and three bogeys in his round of 77 to fall into a tie for 11th place at two over. He took 34 putts while absorbing a stinging blow to his dreams of completing a career Grand Slam this year with a victory here.

    Spieth had 30 putts, which he considered the bright spot.

    “I thought I putted the ball unbelievably well today,” said Spieth, who was less thrilled about his ball-striking. He found seven fairways and 12 greens.

    “I felt much better about my position last year than I do right this second, just because of what happened in the last 40 minutes,” Spieth added.

    Last year, Spieth was four strokes ahead of Justin Rose going into the final round. So the field is catching up. He will be paired on Sunday with Kaufman, who sits alone in second. A Masters rookie, Kaufman finds himself paired with a one-time Masters winner in his first Sunday round, a position similar to what Spieth faced two years ago when he was grouped with the eventual winner, Bubba Watson.

    “I’m just kind of going with the flow and just going to try to play golf and just do the same thing I’ve been doing,” said Kaufman, who shot 61 to win the PGA Tour event this season in Las Vegas.

    Bryson DeChambeau, the 22-year-old amateur who began the day four strokes behind Spieth, made double bogeys on two of the final four holes in an erratic round of 77 (he also had three birdies and four bogeys).

    Joining Spieth, 22, and Kaufman, 24, on the first page of the leaderboard were three other millennials: Hideki Matsuyama, 24; and Danny Willett and the world No. 1, Jason Day, both 28. Matsuyama carded a 72 and is tied for third, two strokes behind Spieth.

    Another stroke back is Day, who shot 71. He was pushed in his round by his playing partner, Bernhard Langer, a former world No. 1 who signed for a 70 and is tied with Matsuyama for third. At 58, Langer is 12 years older than Spieth and Kaufman’s combined ages. He won the second of his two Masters titles in 1993, three months before Spieth was born.

    “I think it’s awesome,” Spieth said. “I would say I’m surprised except for, doesn’t he win most every tournament on the Champions Tour?”

    Can Langer win here 30 years after Jack Nicklaus triumphed at age 46?

    “I believe I can,” Langer said, adding, “I can only play my game and see how that holds up.”

    The last European to win the Masters was José Maria Olazábal in 1999. At the start of the day, five Europeans were within three shots of the front-running Spieth: McIlroy, Soren Kjeldsen of Denmark, Willett of England, Sergio García of Spain and Shane Lowry of Ireland. Their average third-round score was 76.6. On a day when experience was the best guide against the wind, Langer, who had missed the cut seven times in the previous nine years, soared.

    “To be able to be a 58-year-old man, be competitive with us, and want it as much as he did 40 years ago is pretty impressive,” said Day, who thought he had gotten the better of Langer on the 14th when he drained a 69-foot putt for a birdie. Langer responded by chipping in for a birdie.

    “It just goes to show how competitive he is,” said Day, who took one stroke more to get around Augusta National despite outdriving Langer by as much as 60 yards. The key: Langer had 27 putts, which elicited a “well done” from Spieth, who has graced the final pairing in all three Masters he has played.

    He has led seven consecutive rounds and is trying to become the first player since Tiger Woods in 2002 to win back-to-back Masters.

    Davis Love III, the United States Ryder Cup captain, was asked which feat would be more remarkable, Spieth winning consecutive wire-to-wire titles here at age 22, or the Golden State Warriors surpassing the 1995-96 Chicago Bulls’ 72-10 regular-season record.

    “That’s a tough one,” Love said. “Team effort, maybe that’s preferable. But either one would be very, very impressive.”

    An earlier version of this article misstated the number of bogeys Rory McIlroy had in his round. It was three, not two.
    Correction: April 13, 2016
