    Yankees Show They’ve Turned Calendar With Victory Against Blue Jays

    By BILLY WITZAPRIL 12, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    TORONTO — When the Yankees returned here to play the Blue Jays on Tuesday there were a few signs that it was no longer last year. There was no frothy capacity crowd, no David Price and no bat-flipping, swaggering runaway train of a team to greet them.

    There was also a rarity from a year ago: a 3-2 come-from-behind victory.

    The Yankees tied the game on a home run by Brian McCann, took the lead on a bloop single by Jacoby Ellsbury and got a lockdown performance from their bullpen, including a knee-buckling curveball from Dellin Betances to strike out Jose Bautista with the tying run at second base to end the seventh inning.

    There was also good news afterward. Tests on McCann, who left the game at the end of the eighth inning after being hit by a foul tip on his big left toe, were negative.

    “I’m thankful it’s not broke,” said McCann, who injured it in the fifth. “That was the one thing on my mind, basically, the whole rest of the game. I was just hoping it was the blood filling up in my nail and it wasn’t broke.”

    The injury did not seem to hinder McCann’s hitting — he hit a two-out, 3-2 fastball from Aaron Sanchez over the right-field wall in the sixth. Nor did it bother him running — after his eighth-inning single, he raced from first to third on Chase Headley’s base hit.

    “It felt a little bit better,” McCann said when he rounded the bases on his home run. But when it came time to take the field in the ninth, the toe was painful enough that he thought it might hinder his ability to throw.

    “We talked about taking him out after he hit in the sixth and he said, ‘No, I’m good, I’m good, I’m good,’ ” Manager Joe Girardi said. “You know how it is when your nail fills up with blood. It starts pounding and he just felt like he couldn’t push off.”

    McCann’s home run notwithstanding, the Yankees excelled at the little things, taking advantage of Sanchez’s errant pickoff of McCann at second base to score a run on Starlin Castro’s groundout. And the go-ahead run came home after Girardi called for a rare sacrifice bunt: Didi Gregorius bunting Headley and Castro to second and third. That brought the infield in and set up Ellsbury’s blooper over the drawn-in shortstop Troy Tulowitzki.

    The Yankees rarely managed those little things — or the big things — against Toronto last season. When the Blue Jays stormed from eight games behind to overtake the Yankees and win the American League East, they did it largely at the Yankees’ expense, winning nine of the 13 games the teams played over the final two months of the regular season. The Blue Jays threw a 26-inning scoreless streak at the Yankees in August, then bludgeoned them with 30 runs in three straight wins at Yankee Stadium in early September that all but decided the race.

    Over all, the Blue Jays, who had won the season series only one other time since baseball returned to an unbalanced schedule in 2001, won 13 of 19.

    The suggestion that the Blue Jays’ dominance of the Yankees and their late-season surge, which carried them to the verge of the World Series, might have imbued the Blue Jays with a degree of assuredness drew a chuckle from one Yankee.

    “They have a few guys over there who I don’t think have ever lacked confidence,” Headley said with a smile.

    But John Gibbons, in his second stint as the Blue Jays manager, said his team’s chemistry last season set a foundation for the talent to take over. The acquisitions of Russell Martin and Josh Donaldson brought a degree of toughness, an edge that had been lacking. Then the deals in late July for Price, Tulowitzki, left fielder Ben Revere and reliever Mark Lowe transformed them.

    “It wasn’t like we gradually got better,” Gibbons said. “It was like we flipped a switch.”

    Gibbons added: “We’ve had some tough clubhouses in the past. Sometimes it was real fractured, everybody doing their own thing. That all changed. Now it’s totally different — guys talking baseball, talking about hitting with each other. It’s really refreshing.”

    Tanaka may have been refreshed, with Sunday’s scheduled start rained out giving him seven days since his start on opening day. Tanaka, the only pitcher who could scuttle the Blue Jays last season, was ahead either by 0-2 or by 1-2 to eight of the first 10 batters he faced, but yielded three walks and hit a batter. More important, it took him 71 pitches to get through three innings.

    But after a shaky start to the third — he hit Kevin Pillar with an 0-2 pitch, gave up a single to Donaldson and a two-run double to Bautista on a ball that was misplayed by Ellsbury — he escaped further damage and made it through five innings. Johnny Barbato, Chasen Shreve, Betances and Andrew Miller pitched four hitless innings with Barbato getting his first major league win.

    There was one anxious moment. With Betances on with two outs in the seventh, Donaldson stole second and Bautista laid off two curveballs in the dirt from Betances. But the two-time All-Star threw two fastballs on the outside corner that Bautista took for strikes. Then he threw a curveball that left Bautista shaking his head.

    The pitch, Betances admitted, “was as good as it can be.”

    The Yankees could say the same for the start of their season series.
